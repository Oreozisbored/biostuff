## 📘 COMPREHENSIVE STUDY GUIDE & ANSWERS

### Part 1: Chapter Objectives Explanations

#### 2.1–2.4 Elements, Compounds, Atoms, and Subatomic Particles
*   **Matter**: Anything that occupies space and has mass. Exists as solid, liquid, or gas.
*   **Element**: A pure substance that cannot be broken down into other substances by chemical means. 25 elements are essential to life.
*   **Compound**: A substance containing two or more different elements in a fixed ratio (e.g., $H_2O$, $NaCl$). Compounds have emergent properties different from their elements.
*   **Atom**: The smallest unit of matter that retains the properties of an element.
*   **Subatomic Particles**:
    *   **Protons**: Positively charged (+1), located in the nucleus. Number of protons determines the atomic number.
    *   **Neutrons**: Electrically neutral, located in the nucleus. Protons + neutrons determine the mass number.
    *   **Electrons**: Negatively charged (-1), orbit the nucleus in electron shells. Involved in chemical bonding.

#### 2.4 Isotopes and their Uses
*   **Isotopes**: Atoms of the same element that have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons (e.g., Carbon-12, Carbon-14).
*   **Radioactive Isotopes**: Unstable isotopes whose nucleus decays spontaneously, giving off particles and energy.
*   **Uses**: Used as biological tracers to map metabolic processes in organisms, in medical imaging (PET scans), and in carbon dating of fossils.

#### 2.5–2.8 Electron Configurations and Chemical Bonds
*   **Valence Electrons**: Electrons in the outermost shell (valence shell). Atoms interact to fill their outer shell (usually with 8 electrons, the octet rule).
*   **Chemical Bonds**:
    *   **Covalent Bond**: Formed when two atoms share one or more pairs of valence electrons. The strongest chemical bond in biological molecules.
        *   **Nonpolar Covalent Bond**: Electrons are shared equally between atoms of similar electronegativity (e.g., $O_2$, $CH_4$).
        *   **Polar Covalent Bond**: Electrons are shared unequally due to differing electronegativities, creating partial charges (e.g., $H_2O$).
    *   **Ionic Bond**: Formed when one or more electrons are transferred from one atom to another, creating ions (charged atoms). The attraction between oppositely charged ions ($Na^+$ and $Cl^-$) forms the bond.
    *   **Hydrogen Bond**: A weak attraction between a partially positive hydrogen atom in one polar molecule and a partially negative atom (usually oxygen or nitrogen) in another polar molecule. Essential for water's unique properties.

#### 2.9 Chemical Reactions
*   **Chemical Reactions**: The breaking and making of chemical bonds, leading to changes in the composition of matter. Reactants are converted into products. Matter is conserved; atoms are rearranged, not created or destroyed.

#### 2.10–2.14 Life-Supporting Properties of Water
1.  **Cohesion and Adhesion**: 
    *   **Cohesion**: Water molecules stick to each other due to hydrogen bonding. Creates high surface tension.
    *   **Adhesion**: Water molecules stick to other polar surfaces (e.g., plant cell walls). Together, cohesion and adhesion pull water up plant vessels against gravity.
2.  **Temperature Moderation**: Water has a high **specific heat** capacity because hydrogen bonds must absorb heat to break before molecules can move faster. This helps moderate Earth's temperatures and maintain stable internal body temperatures.
3.  **Evaporative Cooling**: As water evaporates, the surface of the liquid remaining behind cools down because the hottest molecules (with greatest kinetic energy) leave as gas. Prevents terrestrial organisms from overheating.
4.  **Ice Floats**: Ice is less dense than liquid water because hydrogen bonds stable into a spacious, crystalline lattice at freezing temperatures. Floating ice insulates the water below, allowing aquatic life to survive winter.
5.  **Universal Solvent**: Water is a versatile polar solvent. It easily dissolves polar and ionic solutes by forming hydration shells around them.

#### 2.15–2.16 pH Scale, Acids, Bases, and Buffers
*   **Acid**: A substance that increases the hydrogen ion ($H^+$) concentration of a solution (pH < 7).
*   **Base**: A substance that reduces the hydrogen ion ($H^+$) concentration of a solution, often by releasing $OH^-$ (pH > 7).
*   **pH Scale**: Logarithmic scale from 0 to 14 measuring $H^+$ concentration. Each pH unit represents a 10-fold change in acidity.
*   **Buffers**: Substances that minimize changes in pH by accepting $H^+$ when it is in excess and donating $H^+$ when it is depleted (e.g., carbonic acid-bicarbonate buffer system in blood).

### Part 2: Key Terms Definitions

*   **Acid**: A chemical compound that releases $H^+$ into solution.
*   **Adhesion**: The attraction between different kinds of molecules.
*   **Aqueous Solution**: A solution in which water is the solvent.
*   **Atom**: The smallest unit of matter that retains the chemical properties of an element.
*   **Atomic Number**: The number of protons in the nucleus of an atom.
*   **Base**: A chemical compound that accepts $H^+$ and removes it from solution.
*   **Buffer**: A chemical substance that resists changes in pH by accepting or donating $H^+$.
*   **Chemical Bond**: An attraction between two atoms resulting from sharing or transferring valence electrons.
*   **Chemical Reaction**: A process leading to chemical changes in matter, involving the making or breaking of chemical bonds.
*   **Cohesion**: The sticking together of molecules of the same kind.
*   **Compound**: A substance containing two or more elements in a fixed ratio.
*   **Covalent Bond**: A strong chemical bond in which two atoms share one or more pairs of outer-shell electrons.
*   **Electron**: A subatomic particle with a single negative electrical charge, orbiting the nucleus.
*   **Electron Shell**: A level of electrons at a characteristic average distance from the nucleus of an atom.
*   **Electronegativity**: An atom's attraction for shared electrons in a covalent bond.
*   **Element**: A substance that cannot be broken down into other substances by ordinary chemical means.
*   **Evaporative Cooling**: The process in which the surface of an object becomes cooler during evaporation.
*   **Hydrogen Bond**: A type of weak chemical bond formed when the slightly positive hydrogen atom of a polar covalent bond in one molecule is attracted to the slightly negative atom of a polar covalent bond in another molecule.
*   **Ion**: An atom or molecule with an electrical charge resulting from a gain or loss of one or more electrons.
*   **Ionic Bond**: A chemical bond resulting from the attraction between oppositely charged ions.
*   **Isotopes**: Elements with the same atomic number but different mass numbers due to differing neutron counts.
*   **Mass Number**: The sum of protons and neutrons in an atom's nucleus.
*   **Matter**: Anything that occupies space and has mass.
*   **Molecule**: Two or more atoms held together by covalent bonds.
*   **Neutron**: A subatomic particle having no electrical charge, found in the nucleus of an atom.
*   **Nucleus**: An atom's central core, containing protons and neutrons.
*   **pH Scale**: A measure of the acidity or basicity of an aqueous solution.
*   **Polar Covalent Bond**: A covalent bond between atoms that differ in electronegativity, pulling shared electrons closer to the more electronegative atom.
*   **Polar Molecule**: A molecule containing polar covalent bonds and having an unequal distribution of charges.
*   **Product**: An ending material in a chemical reaction.
*   **Proton**: A subatomic particle with a single positive charge, found in the nucleus of an atom.
*   **Radioactive Isotope**: An isotope whose nucleus decays spontaneously, giving off radiation.
*   **Reactant**: A starting material in a chemical reaction.
*   **Solute**: A substance that is dissolved in a solution.
*   **Solution**: A liquid that is a homogeneous mixture of two or more substances.
*   **Solvent**: The dissolving agent of a solution.
*   **Surface Tension**: A measure of how difficult it is to stretch or break the surface of a liquid.
*   **Trace Elements**: Elements indispensable for life but required in extremely minute amounts (e.g., Iron, Iodine).
