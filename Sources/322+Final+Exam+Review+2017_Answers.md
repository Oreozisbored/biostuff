## Ch. 1: Biology in the 21st Century

### 1. Vocabulary Definitions
*   **a. Biosphere**: All living things and the places they are found on Earth (includes land, water, and atmosphere).
*   **b. Biodiversity**: The variety of life across the biosphere. Biodiversity generally increases from Earth's poles to the equator due to warmer, more stable tropical climates.
*   **c. Species**: A particular type of living things that can reproduce by interbreeding among themselves and produce fertile offspring.
*   **d. Organism**: Any individual living thing.
*   **e. Cell**: The basic structural, functional, and biological unit of all organisms.
*   **f. Metabolism**: All of the chemical processes that build up or break down materials in an organism to process energy.
*   **g. DNA**: Deoxyribonucleic acid; the double-stranded molecule that stores genetic information in all organisms.
*   **h. System**: An organized group of related parts that interact to form a whole (e.g., body organs, ecosystems).
*   **i. Ecosystem**: A physical environment with different species that interact with one another and with nonliving (abiotic) factors (soil, water, climate).
*   **j. Homeostasis**: The maintenance of constant, stable internal conditions in an organism (e.g., temperature, acidity, solute concentration).
*   **k. Evolution**: The change in the genetic makeup of a population of a species over time.
*   **l. Adaptation**: An inherited genetic trait that gives an advantage to an individual and is passed on to future generations.
*   **m. Observation**: The active acquisition of information from a primary source using the senses or scientific tools.
*   **n. Data**: Observations and measurements recorded during an experiment (can be quantitative or qualitative).
*   **o. Hypothesis**: A proposed, testable, and falsifiable explanation for a scientific question.
*   **p. Experiment**: A controlled procedure carried out to support, refute, or validate a hypothesis.
*   **q. Independent Variable**: The factor that is manipulated or changed by a scientist in an experiment.
*   **r. Dependent Variable**: The factor that is measured or observed to see how it responds to the independent variable.
*   **s. Constant**: Conditions that are kept the same throughout an experiment to ensure only the independent variable is being tested.
*   **t. Theory**: A proposed explanation for a wide range of observations and experimental results that is supported by a wide body of evidence.
*   **u. Microscope**: A scientific instrument used to magnify small objects that cannot be seen with the naked eye.
*   **v. Gene**: A segment of DNA that stores genetic information and codes for a specific protein.
*   **w. Molecular Genetics**: The study of DNA structure and function on a molecular level.
*   **x. Genomics**: The study and comparison of entire genomes, both within and across species.
*   **y. Biotechnology**: The use and application of living things and biological processes for practical, agricultural, or industrial purposes.
*   **z. Transgenic**: An organism that contains genes from another species inserted into its genome (GMOs).

### 2. Conceptual Questions & Answers
*   **2. Define and give examples of earth’s biodiversity.**
    *   *Answer*: Biodiversity is the variety of different life forms on Earth. Examples include the vast range of plant and animal species in a tropical rainforest, marine life in a coral reef, or bacteria in deep-sea thermal vents.
*   **3. Summarize the characteristics that all living things share.**
    *   *Answer*: All living things: (1) Are made of one or more cells; (2) Require a source of energy for metabolism; (3) Respond to environmental stimuli; (4) Reproduce and pass on genetic material (DNA).
*   **5. Summarize four major unifying themes of biology.**
    *   *Answer*: 
        1. **Systems**: Related parts interact to form a whole (from cells to the biosphere).
        2. **Structure and Function**: The shape/design of a biological structure is adapted to its specific function.
        3. **Homeostasis**: Organisms must maintain stable internal environments to survive.
        4. **Evolution**: Genetic adaptation and change over time explain both the unity and diversity of life.
*   **6. Give an example of each of the themes of biology.**
    *   *Answer*: 
        1. *System*: Your heart, blood vessels, and blood cells work together to form the circulatory system.
        2. *Structure/Function*: Human incisors are sharp and flat to cut food, whereas molars are broad and flat to grind food.
        3. *Homeostasis*: Shivering to generate heat when your body temperature drops.
        4. *Evolution*: Orchid mantises evolved colors and shapes that mimic orchid petals to blend in and capture prey.
*   **8. Identify the different elements of scientific inquiry.**
    *   *Answer*: Observation, forming hypotheses, testing hypotheses through controlled experiments, analyzing data, and evaluating results through peer review.
*   **9. Differentiate between theories and hypotheses.**
    *   *Answer*: A hypothesis is a specific, testable prediction for a narrow set of observations. A theory is a broad, highly validated explanation supported by a massive body of diverse evidence.
*   **11. Describe the usefulness of modern imaging technologies.**
    *   *Answer*: Tools like X-rays, MRIs, and CT scans allow non-invasive viewing of internal body structures. Electron microscopes (SEM and TEM) allow imaging of microscopic cell structures in high-resolution 3D or cross-section.
*   **12. Explain the usefulness of computer models in biological systems.**
    *   *Answer*: Computer models simulate complex systems that cannot be studied directly in a laboratory, such as predicting the spread of an epidemic, climate change impacts on ecosystems, or the folding of a complex protein.
*   **13. Summarize how modern computer-based technologies have advanced genetics.**
    *   *Answer*: Computers allow fast sequencing, storage, and comparison of entire genomes (bioinformatics), enabling rapid mapping of disease-causing genes.
*   **15. Evaluate the importance of biology in making informed decisions.**
    *   *Answer*: Biology helps individuals make informed choices regarding personal health (nutrition, disease prevention), environmental issues (conservation, pollution, climate change), and ethical concerns (genetic screening, cloning).
*   **16. Summarize the benefits and risks of the application of biotechnology.**
    *   *Answer*: *Benefits*: Pest-resistant crops, production of life-saving medicines (e.g., insulin), and genetic therapies. *Risks*: Potential environmental impacts of transgenic genes escaping into wild populations, long-term health questions of GMOs, and ethical issues surrounding human gene editing.
*   **17. Explain how advances in technology might affect the future of biology.**
    *   *Answer*: Advanced technologies (like CRISPR gene editing and artificial intelligence) will allow precise correction of genetic defects, personalized medicine, and faster discovery of biological systems.

## Ch. 2: Chemistry of Life

### 1. Vocabulary Definitions
*   **a. Atom**: The smallest basic unit of matter, consisting of protons, neutrons, and electrons.
*   **b. Element**: A pure substance made of only one type of atom that cannot be broken down chemically.
*   **c. Compound**: A substance made of atoms of different elements bonded together in a fixed ratio.
*   **d. Ion**: An atom that has gained or lost one or more electrons, acquiring an electrical charge.
*   **e. Ionic Bond**: A chemical bond formed through the electrical attraction between oppositely charged ions.
*   **f. Covalent Bond**: A strong chemical bond formed when atoms share one or more pairs of valence electrons.
*   **g. Molecule**: Two or more atoms held together by covalent bonds.
*   **h. Hydrogen Bond**: A weak attraction between a slightly positive hydrogen atom and a slightly negative atom (like oxygen or nitrogen) in another polar molecule.
*   **i. Cohesion**: The attraction among molecules of the same substance (e.g., water sticking to water).
*   **j. Adhesion**: The attraction among molecules of different substances (e.g., water sticking to a plant vessel wall).
*   **k. Solution**: A homogeneous mixture of two or more substances.
*   **l. Solvent**: The substance in a solution that is present in the greatest amount and dissolves the solute (usually water).
*   **m. Solute**: The substance that dissolves in a solvent.
*   **n. Acid**: A compound that releases hydrogen ions ($H^+$) in an aqueous solution, having a pH < 7.
*   **o. Base**: A compound that accepts hydrogen ions ($H^+$) or releases hydroxide ions ($OH^-$), having a pH > 7.
*   **p. pH**: A measure of $H^+$ concentration in a solution.
*   **q. Monomer**: A single chemical subunit that serves as the building block of a polymer.
*   **r. Polymer**: A large molecule (macromolecule) made of many monomers bonded together.
*   **s. Carbohydrate**: Organic molecules composed of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen (1:2:1 ratio); includes sugars, starches, and cellulose.
*   **t. Lipid**: Nonpolar organic molecules that are hydrophobic; includes fats, oils, phospholipids, and steroids.
*   **u. Fatty Acid**: Hydrocarbon chains bonded to a carboxyl group; monomers of many lipids.
*   **v. Protein**: An organic polymer composed of amino acids linked by peptide bonds.
*   **w. Amino Acid**: Monomer of proteins; contains an amino group, a carboxyl group, a hydrogen, and a variable R-group.
*   **x. Nucleic Acid**: Polymers made of nucleotide monomers; stores and transmits genetic information (DNA and RNA).
*   **y. Chemical Reaction**: The process of changing substances into different substances by breaking and forming chemical bonds.
*   **z. Reactant**: The starting substances in a chemical reaction.
*   **aa. Product**: The ending substances produced by a chemical reaction.
*   **ab. Bond Energy**: The amount of energy needed to break a chemical bond.
*   **ac. Equilibrium**: The state in a chemical reaction where reactants and products are formed at the same rate.
*   **ad. Activation Energy**: The minimum energy required to initiate a chemical reaction.
*   **ae. Exothermic**: A chemical reaction that releases more energy than it absorbs (usually as heat/light).
*   **af. Endothermic**: A chemical reaction that absorbs more energy than it releases.
*   **ag. Catalyst**: A substance that decreases activation energy and speeds up chemical reactions.
*   **ah. Enzyme**: Biological catalysts (usually proteins) that speed up metabolic reactions in cells.
*   **ai. Substrate**: The specific reactant that binds to an enzyme's active site.

### 2. Conceptual Questions & Answers
*   **2. Identify elements common to living things.**
    *   *Answer*: The four elements that make up 96% of living matter are **Carbon (C), Hydrogen (H), Oxygen (O), and Nitrogen (N)**. Calcium, phosphorus, potassium, and sodium are also essential in smaller amounts.
*   **3. Describe how ions form.**
    *   *Answer*: Ions form when an atom gains or loses electrons to achieve a full outer valence shell. If an atom *loses* electrons, it becomes a positive ion (cation, e.g., $Na^+$). If it *gains* electrons, it becomes a negative ion (anion, e.g., $Cl^-$).
*   **4. Compare ionic and covalent bonding.**
    *   *Answer*: Ionic bonds form when electrons are transferred from one atom to another, creating electrostatic attraction between opposite charges. Covalent bonds form when atoms share pairs of valence electrons. Covalent bonds are generally stronger in biological systems.
*   **6. Recognize the importance of hydrogen bonding.**
    *   *Answer*: Hydrogen bonding holds the two strands of DNA together, stabilizes the 3D structures of proteins, and gives water its unique life-supporting properties (high specific heat, cohesion, adhesion, floating ice).
*   **7. Explain why many compounds dissolve in water.**
    *   *Answer*: Water is a highly polar molecule. Its partially negative oxygen and partially positive hydrogens form hydrogen bonds and hydration shells around polar molecules and ionic compounds, pulling them apart into solution.
*   **8. Compare acids and bases.**
    *   *Answer*: Acids release hydrogen ions ($H^+$) into solution, lowering pH (pH < 7). Bases accept hydrogen ions or release hydroxide ions ($OH^-$), raising pH (pH > 7).
*   **10. Describe the bonding properties of carbon atoms.**
    *   *Answer*: Carbon has four valence electrons, allowing it to form up to four stable covalent bonds with other atoms. It can bond to other carbons to form straight chains, branched chains, or ring structures, building diverse organic molecules.
*   **11. Compare carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids.**
    *   *Answer*:
        *   *Carbohydrates*: Built of monosaccharides; store short-term energy and provide structure (cellulose, chitin).
        *   *Lipids*: Built of glycerol and fatty acids; store long-term energy, form cell membranes (phospholipids), and act as chemical messengers (steroids).
        *   *Proteins*: Built of amino acids; drive chemical reactions (enzymes), form structural tissues (collagen), transport molecules, and support immune defense.
        *   *Nucleic Acids*: Built of nucleotides; store (DNA) and transmit (RNA) genetic instructions.
*   **13. Describe how bonds break and reform during chemical reactions.**
    *   *Answer*: Reactants absorb energy (activation energy) to break existing chemical bonds. The atoms are then rearranged, and new chemical bonds are formed to create the products, releasing energy.
*   **14. Explain why chemical reactions release or absorb energy.**
    *   *Answer*: Reactions release energy (exothermic) if the bond energy of the products is *less* than the bond energy of the reactants. They absorb energy (endothermic) if the bond energy of the products is *greater* than that of the reactants.
*   **16. Explain the effect of a catalyst on activation energy.**
    *   *Answer*: A catalyst lowers the activation energy required to start a reaction, allowing it to occur much faster.
*   **17. Describe how enzymes regulate chemical reactions.**
    *   *Answer*: Enzymes bind specific substrates at their **active sites** using **induced fit**. They strain chemical bonds or hold reactants in precise orientations, lowering activation energy. Enzymes operate under optimal temperatures and pH; their activity is regulated by competitive/noncompetitive inhibitors.

## Ch. 3: Cell Structure and Function

### 1. Vocabulary Definitions
*   **a. Cell Theory**: Landmark theory stating: (1) All living things are made of cells; (2) Cells are the basic unit of structure and function; (3) All cells come from pre-existing cells.
*   **b. Cytoplasm**: The jelly-like fluid (cytosol) and organelles within a cell, excluding the nucleus.
*   **c. Organelle**: A membrane-bound structure that performs a specific function inside a eukaryotic cell.
*   **d. Prokaryotic Cell**: Simple, smaller cells that lack a membrane-bound nucleus and membrane-bound organelles (Bacteria and Archaea).
*   **e. Eukaryotic Cell**: Complex, larger cells containing a membrane-bound nucleus and specialized membrane-bound organelles.
*   **f. Cytoskeleton**: A network of protein fibers (microtubules, intermediate filaments, microfilaments) that supports cell shape, anchors organelles, and enables cell movement.
*   **g. Nucleus**: The double-membraned organelle that stores eukaryotic DNA.
*   **h. Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)**: An extensive network of folded membranes. **Rough ER** contains ribosomes and modifies/folds proteins. **Smooth ER** lacks ribosomes and synthesizes lipids, metabolizes carbs, and detoxifies poisons.
*   **i. Ribosome**: Complexes of rRNA and proteins that synthesize proteins.
*   **j. Golgi Apparatus**: A stack of flattened membranous sacs that modifies, sorts, packages, and ships proteins and lipids from the ER.
*   **k. Vesicle**: Small, membrane-bound sacs that transport materials throughout the cell.
*   **l. Mitochondrion**: Double-membraned organelle that generates ATP through cellular respiration.
*   **m. Vacuole**: Large membrane-bound storage sacs. Plants have a large **central vacuole** to maintain turgor pressure.
*   **n. Lysosome**: Membranous sacs of digestive enzymes in animal cells that break down waste, food, and old cell parts.
*   **o. Centriole**: Cylinder-shaped organelles in animal cells that organize spindle fibers during cell division.
*   **p. Cell Wall**: Rigid outer layer in plants, fungi, and bacteria that protects and supports the cell.
*   **q. Chloroplast**: Organelle in plants and algae that performs photosynthesis, converting light energy into sugars.
*   **r. Cell Membrane**: Selective phospholipid bilayer that controls which materials enter and exit the cell.
*   **s. Phospholipid**: A lipid containing a hydrophilic phosphate head and two hydrophobic fatty acid tails.
*   **t. Fluid Mosaic Model**: Describes the cell membrane as a flexible phospholipid bilayer embedded with a mosaic of proteins that drift laterally.
*   **u. Selective Permeability**: The property of cell membranes that allows some substances (small, nonpolar) to cross easily, while others (large, polar) require active transport or channel proteins.
*   **v. Receptor**: A protein that binds to specific chemical signals (ligands), triggering responses inside the cell.
*   **w. Passive Transport**: The movement of molecules across a membrane without energy expenditure, moving down a concentration gradient.
*   **x. Diffusion**: The passive movement of solutes from an area of higher concentration to lower concentration.
*   **y. Concentration Gradient**: The difference in concentration of a substance across a distance or membrane.
*   **z. Osmosis**: The diffusion of water molecules across a selectively permeable membrane.
*   **aa. Isotonic**: A solution with a solute concentration equal to the inside of the cell (no net water movement).
*   **ab. Hypertonic**: A solution with a higher solute concentration than the inside of the cell (causes water to exit the cell).
*   **ac. Hypotonic**: A solution with a lower solute concentration than the inside of the cell (causes water to enter the cell).
*   **ad. Facilitated Diffusion**: Passive transport of polar molecules or ions across a membrane through specific transport proteins.
*   **ae. Active Transport**: The movement of solutes against their concentration gradient, requiring transport proteins and cellular energy (ATP).
*   **af. Endocytosis**: The active process of engulfing large external materials into vesicles by folding in the plasma membrane.
*   **ag. Phagocytosis**: A type of endocytosis where the cell engulfs large solid particles ("cell eating").
*   **ah. Exocytosis**: The active process of releasing vesicle contents out of the cell by fusing the vesicle with the cell membrane.

### 2. Conceptual Questions & Answers
*   **2. Describe the developments that led to the cell theory.**
    *   *Answer*: Robert Hooke discovered cells in cork (1665). Anton van Leeuwenhoek observed living cells in pond water. Matthias Schleiden stated all plants are made of cells (1838). Theodor Schwann stated all animals are made of cells (1839). Rudolf Virchow proposed that all cells come from pre-existing cells (1855).
*   **3. Differentiate between prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells.**
    *   *Answer*: Prokaryotic cells are smaller ($1-10\ \mu	ext{m}$), lack a nucleus (DNA is in the nucleoid), and contain no membrane-bound organelles. Eukaryotic cells are larger ($10-100\ \mu	ext{m}$), have a membrane-bound nucleus housing DNA, and have specialized membrane-bound compartments (organelles).
*   **5. Describe the internal structure of eukaryotic cells.**
    *   *Answer*: The interior contains the cytoplasm, which houses the nucleus and a variety of specialized membrane-bound organelles suspended in cytosol, all held in place and coordinated by a dynamic network of protein filaments called the cytoskeleton.
*   **6. Summarize the functions of organelles in plant and animal cells.**
    *   *Answer*: 
        *   *Genetic Control*: Nucleus houses DNA; ribosomes synthesize proteins.
        *   *Manufacturing & Distribution*: Rough ER modifies proteins; Smooth ER makes lipids; Golgi sorts/packages; vesicles transport.
        *   *Cleanup & Storage*: Lysosomes digest wastes; vacuoles store water and nutrients.
        *   *Energy Processing*: Mitochondria generate ATP; chloroplasts (plants only) build sugars.
        *   *Structure & Support*: Cytoskeleton provides movement/shape; cell wall (plants only) provides rigidity.
*   **8. Describe the structure of the cell membrane.**
    *   *Answer*: Composed of a double layer of phospholipids (hydrophilic heads face the aqueous exterior/interior, hydrophobic tails face inward). Cholesterol molecules are embedded to regulate fluidity, and transport, receptor, and marker proteins are interspersed throughout.
*   **9. Summarize how chemical signals are transmitted across the cell membrane.**
    *   *Answer*: Chemical signals (ligands) bind to highly specific receptor proteins. **Intracellular receptors** bind to nonpolar signals that pass through the membrane. **Membrane receptors** bind to polar signals on the cell surface, changing shape to trigger a cascade of chemical reactions inside the cell (signal transduction).
*   **11. Describe passive transport.**
    *   *Answer*: Passive transport is the movement of substances down their concentration gradient (from high to low concentration) without utilizing cellular energy (ATP).
*   **12. Distinguish between osmosis, diffusion, and facilitated diffusion.**
    *   *Answer*: Diffusion is the passive movement of any solute down its gradient. Facilitated diffusion is passive movement of polar or charged solutes through specific channel or carrier proteins. Osmosis is specifically the passive diffusion of *water* across a selectively permeable membrane.
*   **14. Describe active transport.**
    *   *Answer*: Active transport is the movement of substances *against* their concentration gradient (from low to high concentration) using carrier proteins and requiring cellular energy (ATP).
*   **15. Distinguish among endocytosis, phagocytosis, and exocytosis.**
    *   *Answer*: Endocytosis is taking large materials into the cell by folding the membrane inward to form a vesicle. Phagocytosis is a specific type of endocytosis where large solid particles are engulfed. Exocytosis is releasing materials out of the cell by fusing a vesicle with the plasma membrane.

## Ch. 4: Cells and Energy

### 1. Vocabulary Definitions
*   **a. ATP**: Adenosine triphosphate; the primary energy-carrying molecule of the cell.
*   **b. ADP**: Adenosine diphosphate; a lower-energy molecule formed when ATP releases a phosphate group.
*   **c. Chemosynthesis**: The process by which some organisms (like deep-sea bacteria) use chemical energy instead of light energy to produce carbon-based food.
*   **d. Photosynthesis**: The process that converts solar energy, water, and carbon dioxide into chemical energy (sugars) and oxygen.
*   **e. Chlorophyll**: The green pigment in chloroplasts that absorbs light energy for photosynthesis.
*   **f. Thylakoid**: Disk-shaped, membrane-bound sacs inside chloroplasts containing chlorophyll; site of the light reactions.
*   **g. Light-Dependent Reactions**: The first stage of photosynthesis that captures solar energy, splits water to release oxygen, and produces ATP and NADPH.
*   **h. Light-Independent Reactions**: The second stage of photosynthesis (Calvin Cycle) that uses ATP, NADPH, and $CO_2$ to build glucose.
*   **i. Photosystem**: Groups of pigment molecules (chlorophyll) and proteins in the thylakoid membrane that capture light energy.
*   **j. Electron Transport Chain**: A series of proteins in a membrane that transfer high-energy electrons, using the energy to pump hydrogen ions and build an electrochemical gradient.
*   **k. ATP Synthase**: A channel protein/enzyme that utilizes the flow of hydrogen ions to phosphorylate ADP into ATP.
*   **l. Calvin Cycle**: The specific chemical pathway in the stroma that fixes carbon dioxide into sugar.
*   **m. Cellular Respiration**: The process that breaks down food molecules (like glucose) in the presence of oxygen to produce ATP, releasing water and carbon dioxide.
*   **n. Aerobic**: Metabolic processes that require oxygen.
*   **o. Glycolysis**: The first step of respiration; an anaerobic process in the cytoplasm that splits glucose into two 3-carbon pyruvates, yielding 2 ATP and 2 NADH.
*   **p. Anaerobic**: Metabolic processes that do not require oxygen.
*   **q. Krebs Cycle**: The second stage of cellular respiration in the mitochondrial matrix that breaks down Acetyl-CoA, releasing $CO_2$ and generating ATP, NADH, and $FADH_2$.
*   **r. Fermentation**: The anaerobic pathway that allows glycolysis to continue by recycling $NADH$ back to $NAD^+$ in the absence of oxygen.
*   **s. Lactic Acid**: The product of fermentation in animal muscle cells and some bacteria, causing temporary fatigue/burning.

### 2. Conceptual Questions & Answers
*   **2. Recognize the importance of ATP as an energy-carrying molecule.**
    *   *Answer*: ATP is the universal energy currency of the cell. Its terminal phosphate bond is easily hydrolyzed to release energy, which is coupled directly to drive energy-consuming cellular work (active transport, muscle contraction, protein synthesis).
*   **3. Identify energy sources used by organisms.**
    *   *Answer*: Sunlight is the primary energy source for photosynthetic producers. Chemical compounds (like glucose, lipids, and amino acids) are the energy sources for heterotrophic consumers. Inorganic chemicals (like hydrogen sulfide) are used by chemosynthetic autotrophs.
*   **5. Relate producers to photosynthesis.**
    *   *Answer*: Producers (plants, algae, photosynthetic bacteria) use photosynthesis to manufacture their own organic food molecules, forming the energetic base of almost all terrestrial and aquatic food webs.
*   **6. Describe the process of photosynthesis.**
    *   *Answer*: Overall, light-dependent reactions capture solar energy in thylakoid membranes, split water to release $O_2$, and load energy carriers (ATP, NADPH). In the stroma, the light-independent reactions (Calvin Cycle) use that ATP and NADPH to reduce $CO_2$ into sugars.
*   **8. Describe the light-dependent reactions in which energy is captured.**
    *   *Answer*: Light excites electrons in Photosystem II. Water is split to replace these electrons, releasing $O_2$. The excited electrons flow down an ETC, pumping $H^+$ into the thylakoid space to drive ATP Synthase. Light also excites electrons in Photosystem I, which are transferred to $NADP^+$ to form $NADPH$.
*   **9. Describe the light-independent reactions in which sugar is produced.**
    *   *Answer*: Occurs in the stroma (Calvin Cycle). $CO_2$ is fixed to RuBP by the enzyme RuBisCO. Using energy from ATP and electrons from NADPH, the intermediate molecules are reduced to form G3P sugars, which are combined to produce glucose.
*   **11. Describe the process of cellular respiration.**
    *   *Answer*: Respiration consists of: (1) Glycolysis (splitting glucose in cytosol to yield pyruvate, 2 ATP, and 2 NADH); (2) Pyruvate Oxidation & Krebs Cycle (in mitochondrial matrix, releasing $CO_2$ and yielding 2 ATP, NADH, and $FADH_2$); (3) Electron Transport Chain & Chemiosmosis (electrons from NADH/FADH2 flow down cristae proteins to oxygen, driving ATP Synthase to generate ~28 ATP).
*   **12. Compare cellular respiration to photosynthesis.**
    *   *Answer*: Photosynthesis *stores* solar energy in glucose, *uses* $CO_2$ and $H_2O$, and *releases* $O_2$. Cellular respiration *releases* chemical energy from glucose to make ATP, *uses* $O_2$, and *releases* $CO_2$ and $H_2O$. The reactants of one are the products of the other.
*   **13. Describe the process of glycolysis.**
    *   *Answer*: Occurs in the cytoplasm anaerobically. Glucose is phosphorylated using 2 ATP, then split into two 3-carbon G3P molecules. These are oxidized by $NAD^+$ and phosphorylated, yielding two 3-carbon pyruvates, 4 ATP (net of 2 ATP), and 2 NADH.
*   **14. Describe the details of the Krebs cycle and the electron transport chain.**
    *   *Answer*: The Krebs cycle takes 2-carbon Acetyl-CoA, combines it with oxaloacetate, and oxidizes it step-by-step to release $CO_2$, loading carriers to produce 6 NADH, 2 $FADH_2$, and 2 ATP per glucose. The ETC takes electrons from these carriers, passes them down membrane proteins to oxygen, and pumps $H^+$ into the intermembrane space. The resulting gradient drives $H^+$ through ATP Synthase to make ~28 ATP.
*   **16. Describe the process of fermentation.**
    *   *Answer*: In lactic acid fermentation (muscles), pyruvate from glycolysis is reduced directly by $NADH$ to form lactic acid, regenerating $NAD^+$. In alcoholic fermentation (yeast), pyruvate is converted to acetaldehyde (releasing $CO_2$), which is then reduced by $NADH$ to form ethanol and regenerate $NAD^+$.
*   **17. Summarize the importance of fermentation.**
    *   *Answer*: Fermentation allows glycolysis to continue producing a small amount of ATP (2 ATP per glucose) in the complete absence of oxygen, by providing a pathway to regenerate the essential electron carrier $NAD^+$.

## Ch. 5: Cell Growth and Division

### 1. Vocabulary Definitions
*   **a. Cell Cycle**: The regulated sequence of growth, DNA replication, and division in eukaryotic cells.
*   **b. Mitosis**: The division of the cell nucleus, segregating duplicated chromosomes into two identical sets.
*   **c. Cytokinesis**: The division of the cell cytoplasm to form two distinct daughter cells.
*   **d. Chromosome**: A single long condensed molecule of DNA wrapped around histones.
*   **e. Histone**: Proteins around which eukaryotic DNA winds, helping to pack and organize chromatin.
*   **f. Chromatin**: DNA in its loose, uncoiled state during interphase.
*   **g. Chromatid**: One of the two identical halves of a duplicated chromosome.
*   **h. Centromere**: The constricted region where sister chromatids are held together.
*   **i. Telomere**: Repetitive, non-coding DNA caps at the ends of chromosomes that protect them from degradation.
*   **j. Prophase**: First stage of mitosis; chromatin condenses into chromosomes, spindle forms, and nuclear envelope breaks down.
*   **k. Metaphase**: Second stage; chromosomes align double-file or single-file along the cell equator (metaphase plate).
*   **l. Anaphase**: Third stage; sister chromatids are pulled apart toward opposite poles of the cell.
*   **m. Telophase**: Fourth stage; nuclear envelopes reform around chromosomes at each pole, chromosomes uncoil, and spindle breaks down.
*   **n. Growth Factor**: Chemical signals (proteins) that stimulate cell division.
*   **o. Apoptosis**: Programmed cell death.
*   **p. Cancer**: Uncontrolled cell division caused by mutations in cell cycle regulatory genes.
*   **q. Benign**: A non-cancerous tumor that remains clustered at its original site.
*   **r. Malignant**: A cancerous tumor whose cells can invade surrounding tissues.
*   **s. Metastasize**: The spread of cancer cells from their original site to other parts of the body.
*   **t. Carcinogen**: Substances known to promote or cause cancer mutations (e.g., tobacco, asbestos, UV radiation).
*   **u. Asexual Reproduction**: The production of genetically identical offspring from a single parent.
*   **v. Binary Fission**: Asexual reproduction in single-celled prokaryotes.
*   **w. Tissue**: A cooperative group of similar cells working together to perform a specific function.
*   **x. Organ**: A structure composed of different tissues working together to perform a task.
*   **y. Organ System**: A group of organs cooperating to carry out a vital body function.
*   **z. Cell Differentiation**: The process by which unspecialized cells develop into mature, specialized cell types with unique structures and functions.
*   **aa. Stem Cell**: Unspecialized cells that can divide to reproduce themselves indefinitely and differentiate into specialized cell types.

### 2. Conceptual Questions & Answers
*   **2. Describe the stages of the cell cycle.**
    *   *Answer*: The cell cycle has two main parts: (1) **Interphase** (growth and preparation): G1 (cell growth and organelle replication), S (DNA replication), G2 (final growth and check); (2) **M Phase**: Mitosis (nuclear division: prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase) and Cytokinesis (cytoplasmic division).
*   **3. Compare rates of division in different cell types.**
    *   *Answer*: Division rates vary based on wear-and-tear. Skin cells and lining of the digestive tract divide rapidly (every few days). Liver cells divide rarely (only to replace damaged tissue). Nerve and muscle cells enter the non-dividing G0 phase permanently.
*   **4. Identify factors that limit cell size.**
    *   *Answer*: The primary limit is the **surface area-to-volume ratio**. As cell size increases, volume increases cubed, while surface area only increases squared. If a cell is too large, its surface area cannot transport nutrients and wastes fast enough to sustain its volume.
*   **6. Describe the structure of a chromosome.**
    *   *Answer*: A chromosome consists of a single linear double-stranded DNA molecule. In preparation for division, DNA is wound around histone proteins into nucleosomes, which pack tightly into condensed chromatin. Duplicated chromosomes consist of two identical sister chromatids joined at a centromere and capped with protective telomeres.
*   **7. Follow chromosomes through the processes of mitosis and cytokinesis.**
    *   *Answer*: (1) *Prophase*: Chromatin condenses, making duplicated chromosomes visible. (2) *Metaphase*: Duplicated chromosomes align single-file at the equator, attached to spindle fibers. (3) *Anaphase*: Centromeres split; sister chromatids are pulled apart to opposite poles. (4) *Telophase*: Chromosomes uncoil, and nuclear membranes reform around them. (5) *Cytokinesis*: Cytoplasm pinches (animal cleavage furrow or plant cell plate) to yield two identical cells.
*   **9. Identify internal and external factors that regulate cell division.**
    *   *Answer*: *External factors* include physical contact (contact inhibition: cells stop dividing when they touch) and chemical signals (growth factors). *Internal factors* include intracellular cyclins and kinases (kinases activated by cyclins, or CDKs) that regulate progression through cell cycle checkpoints.
*   **10. Explain cancer in terms of the cell cycle.**
    *   *Answer*: Cancer is uncontrolled cell division. It occurs when mutations damage genes regulating cell cycle checkpoints (like proto-oncogenes or tumor suppressor genes). As a result, cells divide continuously without receiving growth signals, ignore stop signals, and fail to undergo apoptosis.
*   **12. Compare and contrast binary fission and mitosis.**
    *   *Answer*: Both processes produce genetically identical cells. However, binary fission occurs in prokaryotes (no nucleus, single circular DNA, fast, simple spindle-free separation). Mitosis occurs in eukaryotes (complex, nucleated, multiple linear chromosomes, utilizes a mitotic spindle).
*   **13. Describe how some eukaryotes reproduce asexually through mitosis.**
    *   *Answer*: Multicellular eukaryotes can reproduce asexually via: (1) **Budding**: A new, small organism grows on the body of the parent (e.g., Hydra, yeast); (2) **Fragmentation**: The parent body breaks into pieces, each growing into a new adult (e.g., sea stars, planarians); (3) **Vegetative Reproduction**: Plants growing new plants from stems/roots (e.g., runners in strawberries).
*   **15. Describe specialization in multicellular organisms.**
    *   *Answer*: All cells in an organism contain the exact same DNA. During development, cells undergo **differentiation** where specific genes are turned "on" or "off" in response to chemical signals and position. This gene expression pattern programs the cell to become a highly specialized type (e.g., a red blood cell or a neuron).
*   **16. Identify types of stem cells.**
    *   *Answer*: 
        *   **Totipotent**: Can grow into any cell type + placental tissue (early embryonic cells).
        *   **Pluripotent**: Can grow into any cell type in the body, but not placental tissue (inner cell mass of blastocyst).
        *   **Multipotent**: Can grow only into closely related cell families (adult stem cells, e.g., bone marrow stem cells forming blood cells).

## Ch. 6: Meiosis and Mendel

### 1. Vocabulary Definitions
*   **a. Somatic Cell**: Any diploid ($2n$) body cell in an organism, excluding gametes.
*   **b. Gamete**: Haploid ($n$) reproductive cells (sperm and eggs).
*   **c. Homologous Chromosomes**: A pair of matching chromosomes (one maternal, one paternal) carrying genes for the same traits at the same loci.
*   **d. Autosome**: The 22 pairs of matching chromosomes in humans that do not determine biological sex.
*   **e. Sex Chromosome**: Chromosomes that determine biological sex (X and Y in humans).
*   **f. Sexual Reproduction**: Reproduction involving the fusion of haploid gametes (fertilization) to produce genetically diverse offspring.
*   **g. Fertilization**: The physical union of haploid sperm and egg, forming a diploid zygote.
*   **h. Diploid**: Having two complete sets of chromosomes ($2n$).
*   **i. Haploid**: Having a single set of chromosomes ($n$).
*   **j. Meiosis**: The two-stage cell division process ($2n ightarrow n$) in germ cells that yields four genetically unique haploid gametes.
*   **k. Gametogenesis**: The development of haploid cells from meiosis into mature, functional gametes.
*   **l. Sperm**: The highly mobile, flagellated male gamete.
*   **m. Egg**: The large, nutrient-rich, non-motile female gamete.
*   **n. Polar Body**: Small, non-functional cells produced during oogenesis that disintegrate, allowing one egg to receive almost all the cytoplasm.
*   **o. Trait**: Any distinguishing heritable physical or behavioral characteristic.
*   **p. Genetics**: The scientific study of heredity.
*   **q. Purebred**: Organisms that are homozygous and always pass on their specific traits to offspring (true-breeding).
*   **r. Cross**: The mating of two organisms to study inheritance patterns.
*   **s. Law of Segregation**: Mendel's first law; individuals have two alleles for each gene, and these alleles segregate during gamete formation, so each gamete gets only one.
*   **t. Gene**: A segment of DNA that codes for a specific protein.
*   **u. Allele**: An alternative form/version of a gene.
*   **v. Homologous (Genetics)**: Having identical alleles at a gene locus (synonymous with homozygous).
*   **w. Heterozygous**: Having two different alleles at a gene locus.
*   **x. Genome**: The entire genetic complement/DNA sequence of an organism.
*   **y. Genotype**: The genetic allele combination of an organism (e.g., $BB$, $Bb$, $bb$).
*   **z. Phenotype**: The physical appearance of an organism (e.g., Brown eyes).
*   **aa. Dominant**: The allele that is fully expressed in a heterozygote, masking the recessive allele.
*   **ab. Recessive**: The allele that has no visible effect in a heterozygote and is only expressed when homozygous recessive.
*   **ac. Punnett Square**: A grid diagram used to predict allele combinations and ratios of offspring from a cross.
*   **ad. Monohybrid Cross**: A cross examining the inheritance of only one character.
*   **ae. Testcross**: Mating a dominant phenotype individual of unknown genotype with a homozygous recessive individual to reveal the unknown genotype.
*   **af. Dihybrid Cross**: A cross examining the inheritance of two different characters simultaneously.
*   **ag. Law of Independent Assortment**: Mendel's second law; allele pairs for different genes segregate independently of one another during gamete formation.
*   **ah. Probability**: The mathematical likelihood that a specific event will occur.
*   **ai. Crossing Over**: The exchange of corresponding DNA segments between homologous non-sister chromatids during Prophase I of meiosis.
*   **aj. Genetic Linkage**: The tendency for genes located close together on the same chromosome to be inherited together.

### 2. Conceptual Questions & Answers
*   **2. Differentiate between body cells and gametes.**
    *   *Answer*: Body cells (somatic cells) are diploid ($2n$), meaning they contain two matching sets of chromosomes (46 in humans). They reproduce via mitosis. Gametes are haploid ($n$), containing only one set of chromosomes (23 in humans). They are produced in gonads via meiosis and function in sexual reproduction.
*   **3. Compare and contrast autosomes and sex chromosomes.**
    *   *Answer*: Both are chromosomes carrying genetic information. Autosomes (44 in humans) carry genes for general body traits and are homologous. Sex chromosomes (X and Y) determine biological sex; females are XX (homologous) and males are XY (non-homologous).
*   **5. Compare and contrast the two rounds of division in meiosis.**
    *   *Answer*: **Meiosis I** splits homologous chromosome pairs (reducing ploidy $2n ightarrow n$). It involves synapsis, tetrad formation, crossing over, and double-file metaphase alignment. **Meiosis II** splits sister chromatids (maintaining haploid ploidy $n ightarrow n$). It is identical to mitosis, with chromosomes aligning single-file and centromeres splitting.
*   **6. Describe how haploid cells develop into mature gametes.**
    *   *Answer*: Through gametogenesis. In males (**spermatogenesis**), meiosis yields four equal haploid cells, which develop flagella, lose excess cytoplasm, and package DNA into a dense head to become functional sperm. In females (**oogenesis**), meiosis divides cytoplasm unequally to yield only *one* massive, nutrient-dense egg, and three non-functional polar bodies that disintegrate.
*   **8. Describe the patterns of inheritance that Mendel’s data revealed.**
    *   *Answer*: Mendel's data showed that traits are inherited as discrete units ("factors," now called genes) that do not blend. He showed that some units are dominant and can mask others (recessive) in a predictable $3:1$ ratio in the F2 generation.
*   **9. Summarize Mendel’s law of segregation.**
    *   *Answer*: Organisms inherit two copies of each gene (one from each parent). When gametes form during meiosis, these two copies segregate (separate) so that each gamete receives only one copy.
*   **11. Explain how there can be many versions of one gene.**
    *   *Answer*: A gene is a specific sequence of nucleotides. Small mutations or nucleotide substitutions in that sequence create alternative versions called **alleles** (e.g., gene for flower color has a purple allele and a white allele).
*   **12. Describe how genes influence the development of traits.**
    *   *Answer*: A gene provides the instructions to build a specific protein. That protein then performs a biochemical function (e.g., an enzyme synthesizing a pigment) that directly manifests as a physical trait (phenotype).
*   **14. Describe monohybrid and dihybrid crosses.**
    *   *Answer*: A monohybrid cross tracks one trait (e.g., $Pp 	imes Pp$), yielding a $3:1$ phenotypic ratio. A dihybrid cross tracks two traits (e.g., $RrYy 	imes RrYy$), yielding a $9:3:3:1$ phenotypic ratio.
*   **15. Explain how heredity can be illustrated mathematically.**
    *   *Answer*: Heredity follows the laws of probability. For example, the probability of inheriting a specific allele from a heterozygous parent is $1/2$. The chance of inheriting both recessive alleles ($bb$) is calculated by the product rule: $1/2 	imes 1/2 = 1/4$.
*   **17. Describe how sexual reproduction creates unique gene combinations.**
    *   *Answer*: Sexual reproduction generates genetic variation through three pathways: (1) Crossing over during Prophase I; (2) Independent assortment of homologous chromosomes during Metaphase I; (3) Random fertilization of an egg by a sperm.
*   **18. Explain how crossing over during meiosis increases genetic diversity.**
    *   *Answer*: Homologous chromosomes line up double-file in Prophase I. Non-sister chromatids break and swap matching segments of DNA, creating completely new combinations of maternal and paternal alleles on a single chromosome (recombinant chromosomes).

## Ch. 7: Extending Mendelian Genetics

### 1. Vocabulary Definitions
*   **a. Carrier**: A heterozygous individual who carries a recessive allele for a genetic disorder but does not show symptoms.
*   **b. Sex-Linked Gene**: A gene located on a sex chromosome, usually the X chromosome.
*   **c. X Chromosome Inactivation**: The random turning off of one of the two X chromosomes in female mammalian cells during embryonic development, creating a Barr body.
*   **d. Incomplete Dominance**: Inheritance pattern where the F1 hybrid has an intermediate, blended appearance between the two parents (e.g., Pink snapdragons).
*   **e. Codominance**: Inheritance pattern where both alleles are fully and distinctly expressed in the heterozygote (e.g., AB blood group, speckled chickens).
*   **f. Polygenic Trait**: A physical trait produced by the additive effects of two or more genes (e.g., human skin color, height).
*   **g. Linkage Map**: A map of the relative locations of genes on a chromosome based on crossing-over frequency.
*   **h. Pedigree**: A family tree diagram tracing the inheritance of a trait across generations.
*   **i. Karyotype**: A microscopic image of an individual's complete set of chromosomes arranged in homologous pairs.

### 2. Conceptual Questions & Answers
*   **2. Relate dominant-recessive patterns of inheritance in autosomal chromosomes to genetic disorders.**
    *   *Answer*: Many genetic disorders are autosomal recessive (e.g., Cystic Fibrosis). Only homozygous recessive individuals ($ff$) are affected; heterozygotes ($Ff$) are carriers. Autosomal dominant disorders (e.g., Huntington's disease) affect anyone who inherits at least one dominant allele ($H?$), meaning there are no carriers.
*   **3. Describe patterns of inheritance in sex-linked traits.**
    *   *Answer*: Because males have only one X chromosome (XY), they are hemizygous and will express *any* recessive allele they inherit on their X chromosome (from their mother). Females (XX) must inherit recessive alleles on both X chromosomes to express the trait. Therefore, sex-linked disorders (e.g., colorblindness, hemophilia) are far more common in males.
*   **5. Describe different types of allele interactions.**
    *   *Answer*: 
        *   *Complete Dominance*: Dominant allele completely masks the recessive.
        *   *Incomplete Dominance*: Heterozygote shows an intermediate, blended phenotype.
        *   *Codominance*: Heterozygote expresses both alleles fully and simultaneously.
*   **6. Describe polygenic traits and the effect of environmental factors on phenotype.**
    *   *Answer*: Polygenic traits are controlled by multiple genes, showing continuous variation (bell curve). Phenotype is a product of genotype *and* environment (e.g., human height is determined by genes but limited by nutrition; hydrangea flower color is determined by genes but changed by soil pH).
*   **8. Describe the discovery of gene linkage.**
    *   *Answer*: Thomas Hunt Morgan studied fruit flies (*Drosophila*) and observed that certain traits (like gray body and normal wings) were almost always inherited together. This contradicted Mendel's Law of Independent Assortment, leading to the discovery that genes located close together on the same chromosome are linked and do not assort independently.
*   **9. Explain how linkage maps can be used to estimate distances between genes.**
    *   *Answer*: Alfred Sturtevant reasoned that the further apart two genes are on a chromosome, the higher the probability that crossing over will occur between them. By calculating the recombination frequency (percentage of crossing over), scientists mapped relative gene distances: 1% recombination frequency = 1 map unit (centimorgan).
*   **11. Examine patterns of inheritance in humans.**
    *   *Answer*: Human traits are tracked using pedigree charts. Autosomal recessive traits can skip generations and affect offspring of unaffected parents. Autosomal dominant traits appear in every generation. Sex-linked recessive traits show up predominantly in males and are passed from carrier mothers.
*   **12. Describe how a pedigree is used.**
    *   *Answer*: Pedigrees trace phenotypes and genotypes in a family. By observing affected individuals and their offspring, geneticists determine if a trait is dominant/recessive, autosomal/sex-linked, and calculate the probability of future offspring inheriting a disorder.
*   **13. Identify several methods for mapping human chromosomes.**
    *   *Answer*: Methods include: (1) Analyzing pedigree linkage and recombination rates; (2) **Fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH)** to color specific gene sequences on chromosomes; (3) High-throughput DNA sequencing and bioinformatics.

## Ch. 8: From DNA to Proteins

### 1. Vocabulary Definitions
*   **a. Bacteriophage**: A virus that infects and replicates inside bacteria.
*   **b. Nucleotide**: The monomer of nucleic acids, consisting of a phosphate group, a 5-carbon sugar, and a nitrogenous base.
*   **c. Double Helix**: The molecular shape of DNA, consisting of two parallel strands spiraling around a common axis.
*   **d. Base Pairing Rules**: In DNA, A pairs with T (2 hydrogen bonds), and G pairs with C (3 hydrogen bonds).
*   **e. Replication**: The process by which a cell duplicates its DNA double helix.
*   **f. DNA Polymerase**: The primary enzyme that synthesizes DNA by adding complementary nucleotides to a template strand.
*   **g. Central Dogma**: The unidirectional flow of genetic information: DNA $ightarrow$ RNA $ightarrow$ Protein.
*   **h. RNA**: Ribonucleic acid; a single-stranded nucleic acid containing ribose sugar and Uracil base instead of Thymine.
*   **i. Transcription**: The process of copying a gene's DNA sequence into an RNA transcript.
*   **j. RNA Polymerase**: The enzyme that aligns and links RNA nucleotides during transcription.
*   **k. Messenger RNA (mRNA)**: The RNA transcript that carries genetic instructions from DNA to the ribosome.
*   **l. Ribosomal RNA (rRNA)**: The RNA molecule that, along with proteins, forms the physical structure of ribosomes.
*   **m. Transfer RNA (tRNA)**: The interpreter RNA that carries specific amino acids to the ribosome, matching its anticodon to the mRNA codon.
*   **n. Translation**: The process of decoding mRNA codons into a polypeptide chain of amino acids.
*   **o. Codon**: A three-nucleotide sequence on mRNA coding for a specific amino acid or stop signal.
*   **p. Stop Codon**: Codons (UAA, UAG, UGA) that signal the termination of translation.
*   **q. Start Codon**: The codon (AUG) that initiates translation, coding for Methionine.
*   **r. Anticodon**: The three-nucleotide sequence on a tRNA molecule complementary to an mRNA codon.
*   **s. Mutation**: Any change in the nucleotide sequence of DNA.
*   **t. Point Mutation**: A mutation affecting a single nucleotide base pair (substitution).
*   **u. Frameshift Mutation**: An insertion or deletion of bases that shifts the reading frame of codons.
*   **v. Mutagen**: Physical or chemical agents that cause mutations in DNA.

### 2. Conceptual Questions & Answers
*   **2. Describe Griffith’s discovery of a transforming principle.**
    *   *Answer*: Griffith injected mice with combinations of heat-killed pathogenic S bacteria and living harmless R bacteria. The mice died of pneumonia, and living S cells were recovered, showing a "transforming principle" transferred genetic ability from dead S cells to live R cells.
*   **3. Explain how Avery identified DNA as the transforming principle.**
    *   *Answer*: Avery treated Griffith's extract with enzymes that destroyed either proteins, RNA, or DNA. Transformation of R cells into S cells still occurred normally when proteins and RNA were destroyed, but *failed* when DNA was destroyed, proving DNA was the active material.
*   **4. Summarize the experiments of Hershey and Chase that confirmed DNA as the genetic material.**
    *   *Answer*: They labeled bacteriophages with radioactive isotopes: $^{35}S$ to label viral proteins, and $^{32}P$ to label viral DNA. They allowed the phages to infect bacteria, blended to remove viral coats, and centrifuged. Radioactive $^{32}P$ was found inside the bacterial cells, whereas $^{35}S$ remained outside, confirming DNA is the genetic material injected to reprogram cells.
*   **6. Describe the interaction of the four nucleotides that make-up DNA.**
    *   *Answer*: Nucleotides form a sugar-phosphate backbone via strong covalent phosphodiester bonds. The two strands interact across the helix via weak hydrogen bonds between complementary nitrogenous bases (A with T, G with C), holding the double helix together.
*   **7. Describe the 3D structure of DNA.**
    *   *Answer*: DNA is a double helix composed of two antiparallel polynucleotide strands (one running $5' ightarrow 3'$, the other $3' ightarrow 5'$). The sugar-phosphate backbones form the outer rails, and the paired nitrogenous bases form the inner rungs.
*   **9. Summarize the process of DNA replication.**
    *   *Answer*: Replication is semiconservative: (1) Helicase unwinds the double helix at the origin. (2) Primase lays down RNA primers. (3) DNA Polymerase synthesizes the leading strand continuously ($5' ightarrow 3'$) and the lagging strand discontinuously as Okazaki fragments. (4) Ligase seals the fragments together. (5) Each new molecule contains one old parental strand and one new daughter strand.
*   **10. Describe the role of enzymes in DNA replication.**
    *   *Answer*: *Helicase* unzips the DNA double helix. *Topoisomerase* prevents supercoiling. *RNA Primase* builds RNA primers. *DNA Polymerase III* adds DNA nucleotides to synthesize the new strand. *DNA Polymerase I* replaces RNA primers with DNA. *DNA Ligase* seals nicks in the sugar-phosphate backbone.
*   **12. Describe the relationship between RNA and DNA.**
    *   *Answer*: DNA acts as the permanent master blueprint stored safely in the nucleus. RNA is a temporary, single-stranded copy of a single gene that travels to the cytoplasm to direct protein synthesis. RNA contains ribose sugar (instead of deoxyribose) and Uracil (instead of Thymine).
*   **13. Identify the three kinds of RNA and their functions.**
    *   *Answer*: (1) **mRNA**: Carries genetic messages from DNA in the nucleus to ribosomes in the cytoplasm. (2) **rRNA**: Combines with proteins to build ribosomes, catalyzing peptide bonds. (3) **tRNA**: Decodes mRNA, bringing the correct amino acids to the ribosome based on its anticodon.
*   **14. Compare transcription to replication.**
    *   *Answer*: Both processes occur in the nucleus, unwind DNA, and use complementary base pairing. However, **replication** copies the *entire* genome once to prepare for cell division, using DNA polymerase to yield double-stranded DNA. **Transcription** copies only a *single gene* repeatedly as needed, using RNA polymerase to yield a single-stranded RNA molecule.
*   **16. Describe how mRNA codons are translated into amino acids.**
    *   *Answer*: The ribosome reads mRNA codons one by one. A tRNA with a complementary anticodon binds to the codon at the A site, bringing its attached amino acid. The ribosome transfers the growing polypeptide chain to this new amino acid, forming a peptide bond, and then moves down the mRNA.
*   **17. Summarize the process of protein synthesis.**
    *   *Answer*: *Transcription* copies DNA into pre-mRNA in the nucleus $ightarrow$ pre-mRNA is processed (introns removed, cap/tail added) into mature mRNA $ightarrow$ mRNA travels to a ribosome in the cytoplasm $ightarrow$ *Translation* occurs: tRNAs match codons with amino acids $ightarrow$ amino acids are joined by peptide bonds into a polypeptide chain $ightarrow$ the polypeptide releases and folds into a functional protein.
*   **19. Distinguish between different types of mutations.**
    *   *Answer*: Point mutations (substitutions) swap one base for another; they can be silent (no change), missense (changes one amino acid), or nonsense (creates a stop codon). Frameshift mutations (insertions or deletions) add or remove bases, shifting the reading frame of all subsequent codons and completely changing the protein.
*   **20. Explain why mutations may or may not affect phenotype.**
    *   *Answer*: A mutation may not affect phenotype if it is *silent* (codes for the same amino acid) or occurs in a non-coding intron. It *will* affect phenotype if it changes a key amino acid in an enzyme's active site or creates a premature stop codon (nonsense), resulting in a nonfunctional protein.
*   **21. List some factors that cause mutations.**
    *   *Answer*: DNA replication errors by DNA polymerase. Physical mutagens like UV radiation, X-rays, and gamma rays. Chemical mutagens like cigarette smoke chemicals, asbestos, and reactive oxygen species.

## Ch. 9: Frontiers of Biotechnology

### 1. Vocabulary Definitions
*   **a. Restriction Enzyme**: Enzymes (isolated from bacteria) that cut DNA molecules at specific nucleotide sequences called restriction sites.
*   **b. Gel Electrophoresis**: A laboratory method that uses an electrical current to separate DNA fragments in a gel based on their size (length).
*   **c. Restriction Map**: A map showing the sizes and locations of DNA fragments produced by restriction enzymes.
*   **d. Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)**: A technique used to rapidly make millions of copies of a specific DNA sequence in vitro.
*   **e. Primer**: A short segment of DNA that acts as a starting point for DNA polymerase during PCR.
*   **f. DNA Fingerprint**: A unique representation of an individual's DNA profile based on highly variable regions of non-coding DNA.
*   **g. Clone**: A genetically identical copy of a gene, cell, or organism.
*   **h. Genetic Engineering**: The direct manipulation of an organism's genes for practical purposes.
*   **i. Recombinant DNA**: DNA that contains genes or segments combined from two or more different sources/species.
*   **j. Plasmid**: Small, circular DNA molecules in bacteria that replicate independently of the main bacterial chromosome.
*   **k. Transgenic**: An organism containing inserted genes from another species.
*   **l. Gene Knockout**: A genetic engineering method that disrupts or turns off a specific gene to study its function.
*   **m. Genomics**: The study of entire genomes, gene mapping, and gene comparisons.
*   **n. Gene Sequencing**: The process of determining the exact order of nucleotides in a gene or genome.
*   **o. Human Genome Project**: An international scientific effort (completed in 2003) that sequenced the entire human genome.
*   **p. Bioinformatics**: The use of computer databases and algorithms to store, analyze, and compare massive biological data (DNA sequences).
*   **q. DNA Microarray**: A glass slide containing thousands of tiny DNA spots used to monitor the expression of thousands of genes simultaneously.
*   **r. Proteomics**: The study of all the proteins produced by an organism's genome.
*   **s. Genetic Screening**: The process of testing DNA to determine a person's risk of having or passing on a genetic disorder.
*   **t. Gene Therapy**: A experimental technique that inserts functional genes into a patient's cells to replace or correct a faulty, disease-causing gene.

### 2. Conceptual Questions & Answers
*   **2. Summarize how restriction enzymes cut DNA.**
    *   *Answer*: Restriction enzymes bind to specific double-stranded DNA palindromic sequences (restriction sites). They cut phosphodiester bonds, sometimes leaving straight cuts (**blunt ends**) or staggered cuts that leave exposed single-stranded tails (**sticky ends**). Sticky ends can easily hydrogen-bond to matching sticky ends.
*   **3. Explain how restriction maps show the lengths of DNA fragments.**
    *   *Answer*: Cut DNA is loaded into a gel electrophoresis rig. Negative DNA molecules move toward the positive electrode. Smaller fragments move faster and further through the gel pores, while larger fragments move slower and stay close to the starting wells. Comparing the bands to a DNA ladder reveals fragment sizes.
*   **5. Describe the role of polymerases in copying DNA fragments.**
    *   *Answer*: In PCR, a heat-resistant DNA polymerase (like **Taq polymerase** from hot-spring bacteria) synthesizes new DNA complementary strands, copying the target DNA sequence repeatedly without denaturing at boiling temperatures.
*   **6. Outline the 3-step process of PCR.**
    *   *Answer*: (1) **Denaturing** (95°C): Heat separates the DNA double helix into single strands. (2) **Annealing** (55°C): Temperature is lowered, allowing primers to bind to target DNA sequences. (3) **Extension** (72°C): Taq polymerase synthesizes complementary DNA strands starting from the primers. This cycle is repeated 20-30 times.
*   **8. Describe what a DNA fingerprint represents.**
    *   *Answer*: A DNA fingerprint represents a band pattern of an individual's variable non-coding DNA regions, specifically **Short Tandem Repeats (STRs)**. The number of repeats varies greatly between individuals, making the gel band pattern unique.
*   **9. Summarize how DNA fingerprints are used for identification.**
    *   *Answer*: DNA is extracted from a sample (crime scene, paternity case), amplified via PCR, cut with restriction enzymes, and run on a gel. The resulting band pattern is compared. A perfect match of multiple STR loci confirms identity in forensics, or a combination of maternal and paternal bands confirms paternity.
*   **11. Describe how organisms are cloned.**
    *   *Answer*: Through **Somatic Cell Nuclear Transfer (SCNT)**: (1) An egg cell's nucleus is removed (enucleated). (2) A somatic nucleus from the donor organism is inserted into the empty egg. (3) An electric shock stimulates the egg to divide into an embryo. (4) The embryo is implanted into a surrogate mother, who gives birth to a genetic clone of the donor.
*   **12. Explain how new genes can be added to an organism’s DNA.**
    *   *Answer*: (1) The target gene and a bacterial plasmid are cut with the same restriction enzyme, leaving matching sticky ends. (2) The gene and plasmid are mixed; sticky ends anneal, and DNA Ligase seals them to create **recombinant DNA**. (3) The plasmid is inserted into bacteria (transformation) or cells, which read the new gene and synthesize the foreign protein.
*   **14. Describe genomics.**
    *   *Answer*: Genomics is the comprehensive study of an organism's entire set of genes (genome), mapping gene locations, sequencing bases, and comparing genomes between species to understand evolutionary relationships and disease mechanisms.
*   **15. Identify how technology helps compare and study genes and proteins.**
    *   *Answer*: *Bioinformatics* databases allow rapid computer comparison of DNA sequences. *DNA Microarrays* show which genes are actively turned "on" in cancer cells versus normal cells. *Mass Spectrometry* allows rapid sequencing and analysis of proteins (proteomics).
*   **17. Explain how genetic screening can detect genetic disorders.**
    *   *Answer*: Genetic screening uses PCR and DNA sequencing to look for specific disease-causing gene mutations (like the BRCA gene for breast cancer or CFTR gene for cystic fibrosis) in an individual's DNA, helping determine carrier status or disease risk.
*   **18. Describe how gene therapy research seeks to replace faulty genes.**
    *   *Answer*: Scientists package a functional, healthy copy of a gene inside a harmless virus vector. The virus infects the patient's cells (often bone marrow cells), delivering the healthy gene into the cell's genome. The cells can then synthesize the missing, functional protein, correcting the genetic disorder.

## Ch. 10: Principles of Evolution

### 1. Vocabulary Definitions
*   **a. Evolution**: The process of biological change by which descendants come to differ from their ancestors over time.
*   **b. Species**: A group of organisms so similar to one another that they can breed and produce fertile offspring.
*   **c. Fossil**: Traces or preserved remains of organisms that lived in the past.
*   **d. Catastrophism**: The theory that natural disasters (floods, volcanic eruptions) shaped Earth's landforms and caused mass extinctions in short periods of time.
*   **e. Gradualism**: The theory that Earth's landforms were shaped by slow, gradual processes (erosion, sedimentation) operating over long periods of time.
*   **f. Uniformitarianism**: The theory that the geological processes that shape Earth today have occurred at a constant, uniform rate throughout history.
*   **g. Variation**: Differences in physical traits of an individual from other individuals of the same population.
*   **h. Speciation**: The evolution of two or more species from an ancestral species.
*   **i. Artificial Selection**: The process by which humans breed plants or animals for specific, desirable traits (selective breeding).
*   **j. Heritability**: The ability of a trait to be passed down from parent to offspring genetically.
*   **k. Natural Selection**: A mechanism by which individuals that have inherited beneficial adaptations show greater reproductive success, passing those traits to offspring.
*   **l. Population**: All the individuals of a species that live in a specific geographic area.
*   **m. Fitness**: A measure of the ability of an organism to survive and produce more offspring relative to other members of the population.
*   **n. Biogeography**: The study of the distribution of organisms around the world.
*   **o. Homologous Structures**: Body parts in different species that are similar in structure because of shared evolutionary ancestry, though they may have different functions (e.g., human arm and whale flipper).
*   **p. Analogous Structures**: Body parts that share a common function but developed independently due to similar environmental pressures, not shared ancestry (e.g., butterfly wing and bird wing).
*   **q. Vestigial Structures**: Remnants of organs or structures that served a function in an ancestral species but are now reduced and serve no functional purpose (e.g., pelvic bones in snakes).
*   **r. Paleontology**: The scientific study of fossils and prehistoric life forms.

### 2. Conceptual Questions & Answers
*   **2. Examine early ideas about evolution.**
    *   *Answer*: Carolus Linnaeus developed a classification system, proposing hybridization could create new species. Georges-Louis Leclerc de Buffon proposed species shared common ancestors and Earth was much older than 6,000 years. Jean-Baptiste Lamarck proposed species evolved toward perfection, but incorrectly argued this occurred through the inheritance of acquired characteristics (use/disuse during an organism's lifetime).
*   **3. Identify 3 geological theories that influenced scientific debate over evolution.**
    *   *Answer*: (1) **Catastrophism** (Georges Cuvier); (2) **Gradualism** (James Hutton); (3) **Uniformitarianism** (Charles Lyell). Lyell's uniformitarianism strongly influenced Darwin, showing that slow, gradual processes could cause massive changes over an extremely ancient Earth.
*   **5. Describe how Darwin arrived at his idea about species variation.**
    *   *Answer*: During his voyage on the HMS Beagle, Darwin observed species variation across different environments. In the Galapagos, he noted that finch species had beaks adapted to their specific food sources (strong beaks for cracking seeds; thin beaks for capturing insects), indicating species adapt to their specific local environments over time.
*   **6. Recognize how Darwin’s discoveries supported Lyell’s ancient-Earth theory.**
    *   *Answer*: Darwin found marine fossils high up in the Andes mountains and experienced an earthquake that lifted the shoreline. He reasoned that if slow geological forces could lift mountains over massive timescales (supporting Lyell), then slow biological forces could gradually adapt species over those same massive timescales.
*   **8. Compare artificial selection to natural selection.**
    *   *Answer*: In **artificial selection**, *humans* act as the selective agent, deciding which traits are desirable and breeding individuals with those traits. In **natural selection**, the *environment* acts as the selective agent; individuals with traits best suited to local conditions survive and reproduce naturally.
*   **9. Examine the factors Darwin considered in forming his theory of natural selection.**
    *   *Answer*: Darwin considered: (1) Thomas Malthus's idea that resources limit population growth, leading to competition; (2) His own observations of variation and adaptation in the wild; (3) The heritability of traits demonstrated in artificial breeding.
*   **10. Summarize the four principles of natural selection.**
    *   *Answer*: 
        1. **Variation**: Differences in alleles exist in every population.
        2. **Overproduction**: More offspring are born than can survive, creating competition.
        3. **Adaptation**: Certain variations give individuals a survival advantage.
        4. **Descent with Modification**: Over time, beneficial traits accumulate in populations, changing allele frequencies.
*   **12. Recognize the major sources of evidence of evolution.**
    *   *Answer*: 
        *   *Fossils*: Record transitional species changes over time.
        *   *Biogeography*: Shows species distribution reflects migration from common ancestors.
        *   *Embryology*: Shows diverse species have nearly identical early embryonic stages, indicating shared ancestry.
        *   *Anatomy*: Homologous and vestigial structures reveal modified ancestral plans.
*   **13. Examine the pattern of features that reveal the history of species.**
    *   *Answer*: Vestigial structures (like the flightless wings of ostriches) and homologous limb bones show that evolution does not build new structures from scratch; instead, it modifies existing ancestral anatomical features to serve new functions.
*   **15. Summarize different types of evidence that support evolution.**
    *   *Answer*: Incorporates the anatomical and fossil evidence, backed heavily by modern **molecular evidence**: DNA sequence comparisons, pseudogenes (inactive genes that persist across species), and highly conserved homeobox (Hox) genes that control embryonic development in almost all animals.
*   **16. Recognize the importance of evolution in unifying all branches of biological study.**
    *   *Answer*: Evolution is the core unifying concept of biology. As Theodosius Dobzhansky famously wrote, "Nothing in biology makes sense except in the light of evolution." It ties together genetics, anatomy, development, ecology, and biochemistry.

## Ch. 11: The Evolution of Populations

### 1. Vocabulary Definitions
*   **a. Gene Pool**: The collection of all alleles of all individuals in a population.
*   **b. Allele Frequency**: The proportion of a specific allele in a gene pool.
*   **c. Normal Distribution**: A bell-shaped frequency distribution of phenotypes where the average is most common and extremes are rare.
*   **d. Microevolution**: The observable change in the allele frequencies of a population over generations.
*   **e. Directional Selection**: Natural selection that shifts a phenotypic distribution in favor of one extreme phenotype (e.g., antibiotic resistance).
*   **f. Stabilizing Selection**: Natural selection that favors intermediate phenotypes, acting against both extremes (e.g., human birth weight).
*   **g. Disruptive Selection**: Natural selection that favors *both* extreme phenotypes, acting against the average (e.g., male salmon size).
*   **h. Gene Flow**: The movement of alleles between populations due to migration.
*   **i. Genetic Drift**: A change in allele frequencies due to chance events, primarily affecting small populations.
*   **j. Bottleneck Effect**: Genetic drift occurring after a disaster drastically reduces population size, leaving a non-representative gene pool.
*   **k. Founder Effect**: Genetic drift occurring when a small number of colonizers establish a new population in an isolated area.
*   **l. Sexual Selection**: Selection in which certain traits increase mating success (intersexual or intrasexual).
*   **m. Reproductive Isolation**: Biological barriers that prevent members of two populations from interbreeding or producing fertile offspring.
*   **n. Behavioral Isolation**: Reproductive barrier based on differences in courtship behavior or mating calls.
*   **o. Geographical Isolation**: Reproductive barrier based on physical geographic barriers (canyons, rivers, mountains).
*   **p. Temporal Isolation**: Reproductive barrier based on timing, where populations breed at different times, seasons, or years.
*   **q. Convergent Evolution**: Evolution toward similar traits in unrelated species due to similar environmental pressures (analogous traits).
*   **r. Divergent Evolution**: Evolution toward different traits in closely related species, leading to speciation.
*   **s. Coevolution**: Two or more species evolving in response to changes in each other (e.g., predator-prey arms race, pollinator-flower mutualism).
*   **t. Extinction**: The complete elimination of a species from Earth.
*   **u. Punctuated Equilibrium**: The theory that species undergo long periods of little change (stasis) interrupted by brief periods of rapid evolutionary change.
*   **v. Adaptive Radiation**: The rapid speciation of one ancestral species into many diverse descendant species, typically to fill empty ecological niches.

### 2. Conceptual Questions & Answers
*   **2. Describe the significance of genetic variation within a population.**
    *   *Answer*: Genetic variation is essential because it provides the raw phenotypic diversity upon which natural selection acts. A genetically diverse population is far more likely to survive environmental changes (diseases, climate shifts) because some individuals will possess alleles that allow them to adapt.
*   **3. Identify sources of genetic variation.**
    *   *Answer*: The primary source of new alleles is **mutation** (random DNA changes). Sexual reproduction reshuffles existing alleles through **recombination** (crossing over, independent assortment during meiosis) and random fertilization.
*   **5. Describe how natural selection acts on the distribution of traits in a population.**
    *   *Answer*: Natural selection acts on the phenotypic distribution. If an environment is stable, it preserves the average phenotype. If the environment changes or resource competition increases, it selects against certain phenotypes, shifting the distribution.
*   **6. Explain three ways natural selection can change the distribution of a trait.**
    *   *Answer*: 
        1. **Directional Selection**: Favors one extreme, shifting the curve left or right.
        2. **Stabilizing Selection**: Favors the average, narrowing the bell curve and eliminating both extremes.
        3. **Disruptive Selection**: Favors both extremes, creating a bimodal (double-peaked) curve.
*   **8. Explain how gene flow, genetic drift, and sexual selection lead to the evolution of populations.**
    *   *Answer*: *Gene flow* adds or removes alleles, keeping neighboring gene pools similar. *Genetic drift* randomly changes allele frequencies due to chance, reducing genetic variation. *Sexual selection* selects for traits that increase mating success (even if they decrease survival, like a peacock's heavy tail), changing allele frequencies rapidly.
*   **10. Explain how isolation of populations can lead to speciation.**
    *   *Answer*: When two populations of a species become reproductively isolated, gene flow between them stops. The two gene pools undergo natural selection, genetic drift, and mutations independently. Over time, genetic differences accumulate until they can no longer interbreed, achieving speciation.
*   **11. Describe how populations can become isolated.**
    *   *Answer*: Populations isolate via: (1) **Geographic Isolation** (rivers, canyons, islands); (2) **Behavioral Isolation** (unrecognized song/dance); (3) **Temporal Isolation** (nocturnal vs. diurnal, spring vs. fall mating).
*   **13. Describe different types and rates of evolution.**
    *   *Answer*: Unrelated species undergo *convergent evolution* to look similar. Related species undergo *divergent evolution* to look different. Coevolving species adapt in sync. Rates of evolution can be slow and steady (*gradualism*) or show long stasis broken by rapid bursts (*punctuated equilibrium*).
*   **14. Compare different types and rates of extinction.**
    *   *Answer*: **Background Extinction** occurs continuously at a low rate due to local environmental changes, affecting one species at a time. **Mass Extinction** occurs suddenly at a global level due to catastrophic events (asteroid impacts, ice ages), wiping out entire taxonomic families and thousands of species simultaneously.

## Ch. 13: Principles of Ecology

### 1. Vocabulary Definitions
*   **a. Ecology**: The scientific study of interactions among organisms and between organisms and their physical environment.
*   **b. Community**: An interacting group of various species living in a common location.
*   **c. Ecosystem**: A biological community of interacting organisms and their physical, nonliving (abiotic) environment.
*   **d. Biome**: A major regional or global community of organisms characterized by its climate and vegetation (e.g., Tundra, Rainforest).
*   **e. Biotic**: Living factors in an ecosystem (plants, animals, decomposers).
*   **f. Abiotic**: Nonliving physical and chemical factors in an ecosystem (temperature, water, wind, soil, rocks).
*   **g. Biodiversity**: The variety of living things in an ecosystem.
*   **h. Keystone Species**: A species that has an unusually large effect on its ecosystem relative to its biomass (e.g., Sea otters, Beavers).
*   **i. Producer**: Autotrophic organisms that make their own organic food from inorganic sources (photosynthesis or chemosynthesis).
*   **j. Autotroph**: An organism that manufactures its own food.
*   **k. Consumer**: Heterotrophic organisms that obtain energy by feeding on other living or once-living things.
*   **l. Heterotroph**: An organism that must consume other organic matter for energy.
*   **m. Chemosynthesis**: The process of using chemical energy (inorganic molecules) to synthesize carbohydrates.
*   **n. Food Chain**: A single, linear sequence of feeding relationships showing transfer of energy up trophic levels.
*   **o. Carnivore**: An organism that eats only animals.
*   **p. Detritivore**: An organism that eats detritus (dead organic matter).
*   **q. Specialist**: A consumer that primarily eats only one specific organism (e.g., Koala eating eucalyptus).
*   **r. Trophic Level**: The position or feeding level an organism occupies in a food chain.
*   **s. Herbivore**: An organism that eats only plants.
*   **t. Omnivore**: An organism that eats both plants and animals.
*   **u. Decomposer**: Detritivores that break down dead organic matter into simpler inorganic compounds, recycling nutrients (fungi, bacteria).
*   **v. Generalist**: A consumer with a highly diverse diet (e.g., raccoon).
*   **w. Food Web**: A complex network of branching, interconnected food chains in an ecosystem.
*   **x. Hydrologic Cycle**: The continuous cycle of water evaporating, condensing, falling as precipitation, and returning to water bodies.
*   **y. Biogeochemical Cycle**: The chemical pathways through which matter (carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus) cycles through the biosphere.
*   **z. Nitrogen Fixation**: The process by which specific bacteria convert atmospheric nitrogen gas ($N_2$) into ammonium ($NH_4^+$) which plants can absorb.
*   **aa. Biomass**: The total dry mass of organic matter in a given area.
*   **ab. Energy Pyramid**: A diagram showing the cumulative loss of energy at each ascending trophic level.

### 2. Conceptual Questions & Answers
*   **2. Summarize the levels of organization that ecologists study.**
    *   *Answer*: Ecologists study life at five ascending levels: (1) **Organism** (individual); (2) **Population** (same species in an area); (3) **Community** (different species in an area); (4) **Ecosystem** (community + abiotic factors); (5) **Biome** (major regional climate zone).
*   **3. Describe research methods ecologists use to study the environment.**
    *   *Answer*: Methods include: (1) **Observation** (direct/indirect monitoring, tracking); (2) **Experimentation** (lab tests or field manipulations); (3) **Modeling** (computer simulations to predict environmental changes).
*   **5. Identify biotic and abiotic factors in an ecosystem.**
    *   *Answer*: *Biotic*: Plants, herbivores, carnivores, fungi, bacteria, insect pollinators. *Abiotic*: Air temperature, rainfall, sunlight intensity, soil acidity, wind, rocks, water currents.
*   **6. Describe how a change in one factor in an ecosystem can affect others.**
    *   *Answer*: Ecosystem factors are interconnected. For example, if a drought occurs (abiotic change), plant populations drop (biotic), leading to starvation of herbivores, which in turn causes predator populations to crash. Removing a **keystone species** (like sea otters) leads to an explosion of sea urchins, which completely destroy kelp forests, eliminating habitats for dozens of other species.
*   **8. Describe the role of producers and consumers in ecosystems.**
    *   *Answer*: **Producers** act as the energetic entry point, capturing inorganic energy (solar/chemical) to create chemical sugars. **Consumers** disperse that energy through food webs by eating producers and other consumers, while **decomposers** recycle the nutrients back to the producers.
*   **9. Compare photosynthesis to chemosynthesis.**
    *   *Answer*: Both are autotrophic processes that synthesize organic sugars from carbon dioxide. However, **photosynthesis** uses *light energy* and water, releasing oxygen. **Chemosynthesis** uses *chemical energy* from inorganic molecules (like hydrogen sulfide) and does not require light, occurring in deep-sea vents or caves.
*   **11. Describe the structure of a food chain.**
    *   *Answer*: A food chain is structured vertically by trophic levels: Producers $ightarrow$ Primary Consumers (herbivores) $ightarrow$ Secondary Consumers (carnivores) $ightarrow$ Tertiary Consumers $ightarrow$ Quaternary Consumers. Decomposers operate at every level, breaking down dead matter.
*   **12. Explain how food chains and trophic levels are related.**
    *   *Answer*: A food chain illustrates the flow of energy, where each step or link in the chain represents a specific trophic level.
*   **13. Analyze feeding relationships in a food web.**
    *   *Answer*: Food webs show that many consumers are omnivorous, feeding on multiple trophic levels. Arrows always point in the direction of energy flow (from the eaten to the eater, i.e., "into the stomach"). Removing one node in a web causes ripple effects across multiple food chains.
*   **15. Summarize Earth’s hydrologic and biogeochemical cycles.**
    *   *Answer*: Water cycles globally via evaporation, condensation, and precipitation. Carbon cycles via photosynthesis (absorption) and respiration/combustion (release). Nitrogen cycles via bacterial nitrogen-fixation, nitrification, assimilation by plants, and denitrification.
*   **16. Relate cycling of matter to ecosystems.**
    *   *Answer*: Energy flows one-way through ecosystems and is lost as heat, but **matter must cycle**. Living organisms require carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, and water to build macromolecules; biogeochemical cycles ensure these chemical nutrients are recycled endlessly between living organisms and the abiotic environment.
*   **18. Trace the flow of energy through an ecosystem, using an energy pyramid.**
    *   *Answer*: Solar energy is captured at the base (producers). As energy moves up to primary, secondary, and tertiary consumers, approximately **90% of the energy is lost** as heat or metabolic waste at each level. Only **10% is transferred** to the next level.
*   **19. Relate energy pyramids to food chains and trophic levels.**
    *   *Answer*: Trophic levels correspond to horizontal tiers on the energy pyramid. Because energy is depleted by 90% at each step, ecosystems rapidly run out of energy, explaining why food chains rarely exceed 4-5 trophic levels.
*   **20. Compare and contrast a biomass pyramid and a pyramid of numbers.**
    *   *Answer*: Both illustrate the distribution of resources up trophic levels. A **biomass pyramid** represents the total dry organic mass of all organisms at each level (in grams or kilograms). A **pyramid of numbers** represents the total count of individual organisms at each level. While energy pyramids are always upright, number pyramids can sometimes be inverted (e.g., one large oak tree supporting thousands of insects).

## Ch. 14: Interactions in Ecosystems

### 1. Vocabulary Definitions
*   **a. Habitat**: The physical location where an organism lives, including its biotic and abiotic features.
*   **b. Ecological Niche**: The sum total of all resources used and roles played by a species in its habitat; its "ecological job."
*   **c. Competitive Exclusion**: The principle that two species cannot occupy the exact same niche in the same habitat; the superior competitor will exclude the other.
*   **d. Ecological Equivalent**: Unrelated species that occupy identical niches in different geographic regions (e.g., mantella frogs in Madagascar and poison dart frogs in South America).
*   **e. Competition**: Interspecific or intraspecific interaction where organisms compete for identical limiting resources ($-/-$).
*   **f. Predation**: Interaction where one organism (predator) kills and consumes another (prey) ($+/-$).
*   **g. Symbiosis**: A close, long-term physical relationship between two different species.
*   **h. Mutualism**: Symbiotic relationship where both species benefit ($+/+$).
*   **i. Commensalism**: Symbiotic relationship where one species benefits and the other is unaffected ($+/0$).
*   **j. Parasitism**: Symbiotic relationship where one species (parasite) benefits and the other (host) is harmed ($+/-$).
*   **k. Population Density**: The number of individuals of a species per unit of area or volume.
*   **l. Population Dispersion**: The spatial pattern of individuals within a geographic range (clumped, uniform, or random).
*   **m. Survivorship Curve**: A graph showing the number of surviving individuals from a cohort over time.
*   **n. Immigration**: The movement of individuals into a population from another area.
*   **o. Emigration**: The movement of individuals out of a population to another area.
*   **p. Exponential Growth**: Rapid, unrestricted population growth occurring under ideal, unlimited resource conditions, creating a J-shaped curve.
*   **q. Logistic Growth**: Population growth that slows and levels off as resources become limited, creating an S-shaped curve.
*   **r. Carrying Capacity**: The maximum number of individuals of a species that an environment can support over time.
*   **s. Population Crash**: A sudden, dramatic drop in population size when a population overshoots its carrying capacity.
*   **t. Limiting Factor**: Any environmental factor (resource, threat) that restricts population growth.
*   **u. Density-Dependent Limiting Factor**: Limiting factors whose impact is determined by population size (e.g., competition, disease, predation, parasitism).
*   **v. Density-Independent Limiting Factor**: Limiting factors that affect populations regardless of their size (e.g., natural disasters, weather, human activities).
*   **w. Succession**: The predictable sequence of biotic changes that regenerates a damaged community or creates a new community in a barren area.
*   **x. Primary Succession**: Succession occurring in a completely barren area lacking soil (e.g., bare volcanic rock, retreating glacier).
*   **y. Pioneer Species**: The first organisms to colonize barren rock during primary succession (e.g., lichens, mosses), which break down rock to create soil.
*   **z. Secondary Succession**: Succession occurring in an area where an existing community was cleared by a disturbance (fire, farming) but the soil remains intact.

### 2. Conceptual Questions & Answers
*   **2. Differentiate between habitat and niche.**
    *   *Answer*: A habitat is the *address* of an organism (the physical environment). A niche is the *profession* of the organism, representing all its behaviors, feeding habits, resource use, and interactions within that environment.
*   **3. Differentiate between competitive exclusion and ecological equivalents.**
    *   *Answer*: **Competitive exclusion** states that two species *cannot* share the same niche in the *same* habitat, as one will eliminate the other. **Ecological equivalents** are species that *do* have identical niches but live in *different* parts of the world, so they never compete.
*   **5. Compare and contrast interspecific and intraspecific competition.**
    *   *Answer*: Both involve competition for resources. **Interspecific competition** occurs between members of *different* species (e.g., lions and hyenas competing for prey). **Intraspecific competition** occurs between members of the *same* species (e.g., two male deer competing for mates or territory). Intraspecific is usually more intense because resource needs are identical.
*   **6. Describe three types of symbiosis.**
    *   *Answer*: 
        1. **Mutualism** ($+/+$): Both benefit (e.g., acacia ants protecting the acacia tree).
        2. **Commensalism** ($+/0$): One benefits, other unaffected (e.g., remoras riding on sharks).
        3. **Parasitism** ($+/-$): One benefits, other harmed (e.g., tapeworm in mammal intestines).
*   **8. Consider density and geographic dispersal as characteristics of populations.**
    *   *Answer*: *Density* tells us how crowded a population is, which affects resource availability and disease spread. *Dispersal* shows how individuals are distributed: **clumped** (around resources or in herds), **uniform** (due to territoriality/competition), or **random** (uncommon, occurring when resources are highly abundant and uniform).
*   **9. Describe the three basic types of survivorship curves in relation to reproductive strategies.**
    *   *Answer*: 
        *   **Type I** (Flat start, steep drop at end): High survival in early/middle life, followed by rapid mortality in old age. Associated with **K-selection** (few offspring, high parental care, e.g., humans, elephants).
        *   **Type II** (Linear diagonal drop): Constant mortality rate throughout life (e.g., birds, rodents).
        *   **Type III** (Steep drop at start, flat end): Extremely high infant mortality, but survivors live a long time. Associated with **r-selection** (many offspring, zero parental care, e.g., fish, frogs, insects).
*   **11. Describe four factors that affect population size.**
    *   *Answer*: (1) **Births** (increases size); (2) **Deaths** (decreases size); (3) **Immigration** (increases size); (4) **Emigration** (decreases size).
*   **12. Compare exponential and logistic population growth.**
    *   *Answer*: **Exponential growth** occurs when resources are unlimited, resulting in rapid J-curve growth. **Logistic growth** is more realistic, starting with exponential growth, then slowing down as resources deplete, and finally leveling off at the **carrying capacity** (S-shaped curve).
*   **13. Identify factors that limit population growth.**
    *   *Answer*: Limited by **density-dependent factors** (food, water, territory competition, predator abundance, infectious disease spread) and **density-independent factors** (forest fires, floods, volcanic eruptions, freezing weather, human habitat destruction).
*   **15. Describe the process of primary succession.**
    *   *Answer*: (1) Bare rock is exposed. (2) **Pioneer species** (lichens/mosses) blow in, colonize, and secrete acids that break rock down into dust. (3) Dead lichens mix with rock dust to form thin soil. (4) Windblown seeds of weeds/grasses grow, enriching the soil when they die. (5) Shrub seeds germinate, followed by small fast-growing trees. (6) Over centuries, a stable **climax community** of slow-growing hardwood trees develops.
*   **16. Explain the difference between primary and secondary succession.**
    *   *Answer*: **Primary succession** begins in a completely barren area with **no soil** (bare rock, sand, lava). It is a slow process (centuries) because soil must be built from scratch by pioneer species. **Secondary succession** begins in an area where a community was cleared but the **soil remains intact** (after a fire, logging, or farming). It is a much faster process because the rich soil is already present.
