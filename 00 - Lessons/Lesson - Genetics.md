# 🎲 Lesson 7: Principles of Transmission Genetics and Inheritance

**[[Genetics]]** is the study of heredity and biological variation. The foundation of modern genetics was established by **[[Gregor Mendel]]**, who bred pea plants to discover how discrete units of inheritance are passed down across generations.

---

## 🧬 Mendelian Genetics: Genotype and Phenotype

Mendel discovered that traits are determined by discrete factors, which we now call **[[Gene|Genes]]**. A gene is a segment of DNA that determines a specific heritable **[[Trait]]**:
* Genes exist in alternative forms called **[[Allele|Alleles]]** (e.g., purple vs. white flower alleles).
* An organism's physical appearance is its **[[Phenotype]]**, while its genetic makeup is its **[[Genotype]]**.
* **[[Homozygous]]**: Having two identical alleles for a gene (e.g., $YY$ or $yy$).
* **[[Heterozygous]]**: Having two different alleles for a gene (e.g., $Yy$).
* **[[Dominant Allele]]**: The allele that is fully expressed in a heterozygote, masking the other.
* **[[Recessive Allele]]**: The allele that is masked and only expressed if homozygous.

### Mendel's Laws
1. **[[Law of Segregation]]**: The two alleles for a character separate during gamete formation (meiosis) and end up in different gametes.
2. **[[Law of Independent Assortment]]**: Each pair of alleles segregates independently of other pairs during gamete formation (only applies to genes on different chromosomes).

---

## 📊 Genetic Crosses and Probability

We predict genetic outcomes using a **[[Punnett Square]]**:
* **[[Monohybrid Cross]]**: A cross tracking a single character.
* **[[Dihybrid Cross]]**: A cross tracking two characters simultaneously.
* **[[Testcross]]**: Breeding an individual of unknown genotype with a homozygous recessive individual to determine the unknown genotype.
* Cross calculations rely on **[[Probability in Genetics]]**:
  * *Multiplication Rule*: Probability of two independent events occurring together is the product of their individual probabilities.
  * *Addition Rule*: Probability of any one of two mutually exclusive events occurring is the sum of their individual probabilities.

---

## 🌈 Non-Mendelian Patterns of Inheritance

Many traits display patterns of inheritance more complex than simple dominance:
* **[[Incomplete Dominance]]**: The heterozygote has an intermediate, blended phenotype (e.g., crossing red and white snapdragons produces pink flowers).
* **[[Codominance]]**: Both alleles are fully and independently expressed in the heterozygote (e.g., roan coat color in cattle).
* **[[Multiple Alleles]]**: A gene exists in more than two allelic forms in a population. A classic example is **[[ABO Blood Types]]**, which features three alleles ($I^A$, $I^B$, and $i$) coding for blood cell antigens.
* **[[Pleiotropy]]**: A single gene has multiple, seemingly unrelated phenotypic effects (e.g., sickle-cell disease causing anemia, organ damage, and pain).
* **[[Polygenic Inheritance]]**: Multiple genes act additively to determine a single phenotypic character (e.g., human skin color, height).
* **[[Environmental Effects on Phenotype]]**: The environment can alter gene expression (e.g., hydrangeas changing color based on soil acidity).

## 🧬 Chromosomes, Linkage, and Sex-Linked Traits

In humans, chromosomes consist of 22 pairs of **[[Autosomes]]** and one pair of **[[Sex Chromosomes]]** ($XX$ for females, $XY$ for males).
* **[[Sex-Linked Inheritance]]** refers to genes located on the sex chromosomes. Most sex-linked traits are **[[X-Linked Traits]]** located on the $X$ chromosome.
* Because males have only one $X$ chromosome, they express any X-linked recessive allele they inherit (e.g., hemophilia, red-green colorblindness).
* In female mammals, one of the two $X$ chromosomes is randomly inactivated in each cell during embryonic development, forming a Barr body—a process called **[[X Chromosome Inactivation]]**.

### Genetic Linkage and Mapping
Genes located close together on the same chromosome tend to be inherited together, a phenomenon called **[[Genetic Linkage]]**.
* Linkage is occasionally broken by crossing over.
* Scientists measure recombination frequencies to construct a **[[Linkage Map]]**, mapping the relative distances and positions of genes along a chromosome.

---

## 👪 Human Genetics and Pedigrees

We study human inheritance using a **[[Pedigree]]**—a family tree diagram showing the phenotypes of family members across generations:
* **[[Autosomal Recessive Disorders]]**: Require two copies of the abnormal allele to show symptoms. Heterozygotes are normal but act as a **[[Carrier]]**.
* **[[Autosomal Dominant Disorders]]**: Require only one copy of the dominant disease-causing allele to express symptoms (e.g., Huntington's disease).
