# 🦴 Lesson 10: Evolutionary Theory and Mechanisms

Evolution is the unifying theme of all biology. It explains both the unity of life (common ancestry) and the diversity of life (adaptation to different environments).

---

## 📜 Historical Development of Evolutionary Thought

The idea of evolution did not begin with **[[Charles Darwin]]**, but he proposed the correct mechanism that drives it:
* **[[Lamarck]]** proposed an early theory of evolution based on the inheritance of acquired characteristics, which was later proven incorrect.
* Geologists like Lyell and Cuvier proposed ideas of **[[Uniformitarianism]]** and **[[Catastrophism]]**, which influenced Darwin's thinking about deep time and gradual change.

Darwin's breakthrough was **[[Natural Selection]]**, the process by which individuals with inherited traits best suited to their environment have higher survival and reproductive success (**[[Fitness]]**), passing those traits to their offspring. This leads to **[[Adaptation]]**, heritable traits that improve an organism's ability to survive in its environment.

Humans can drive a similar process called **[[Artificial Selection]]**, selectively breeding plants and animals for desirable traits (e.g., dog breeds, agricultural crops).

---

## 📂 Evidence for Evolution

Modern evolutionary theory is supported by multiple lines of **[[Evidence for Evolution]]**:
* **[[Fossil Record]]**: Chronological collection of life's remains in sedimentary rock layers, showing transitional forms.
* **[[Biogeography]]**: The geographic distribution of species, showing how species adapt to local conditions.
* **[[Homologous Structures]]**: Anatomical structures in different species that share a common evolutionary origin, even if they serve different functions (e.g., human arm and bat wing).
* **[[Vestigial Structures]]**: Remnants of structures that had a function in ancestral species but are reduced or functionless in modern species (e.g., whale pelvic bones).
* **[[Analogous Structures]]**: Structures that perform similar functions but do not share a common evolutionary origin (e.g., insect wings and bird wings).
* **[[Embryology]]**: Shared developmental stages among diverse species.
* **[[Molecular Evidence]]**: Commonalities in DNA, RNA, and protein sequences among related species.

---

## 🧬 Population Genetics and Microevolution

**[[Evolution]]** is a change in the genetic composition of a population over generations. We study this at the level of **[[Microevolution]]**—changes in allele frequencies within a **[[Gene Pool]]** (the total collection of alleles in a population).

If a population is not evolving, it is in **[[Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium]]**, meaning allele frequencies remain constant because:
1. No mutations occur.
2. Mating is random.
3. No natural selection occurs.
4. The population size is extremely large.
5. No gene flow occurs.

The four main mechanisms that alter allele frequencies are:
1. **Natural Selection**: Adapts populations to their environments.
2. **[[Genetic Drift]]**: Random fluctuations in allele frequencies due to chance events (highly pronounced in small populations). Classic examples include:
   * **[[Bottleneck Effect]]**: A severe reduction in population size due to a disaster, leaving a small, genetically non-representative surviving population.
   * **[[Founder Effect]]**: A few individuals colonize a new habitat, establishing a gene pool different from the parent population.
3. **[[Gene Flow]]**: The transfer of alleles into or out of a population due to migration.
4. **[[Sexual Selection]]**: Natural selection for mating success, often leading to sexual dimorphism.

Types of selection on polygenic traits include **[[Stabilizing Selection]]** (favors intermediate phenotypes), **[[Directional Selection]]** (favors one extreme phenotype), and **[[Disruptive Selection]]** (favors both extreme phenotypes).

---

## 🌳 Macroevolution and Speciation

**[[Speciation]]** is the formation of new species, occurring when populations become reproductively isolated. **[[Reproductive Isolation]]** is maintained by:
* **[[Prezygotic Barriers]]**: Prevent mating or fertilization (e.g., habitat, temporal, behavioral, mechanical, or gametic isolation).
* **[[Postzygotic Barriers]]**: Prevent a hybrid zygote from developing into a fertile adult (e.g., hybrid inviability or sterility).

Speciation can be driven by **[[Geographic Isolation]]** (allopatric speciation) or occur in the same area (sympatric speciation).

Patterns of evolution include:
* **[[Adaptive Radiation]]**: The rapid evolution of many species from a common ancestor when colonizing new environments (e.g., Darwin's finches).
* **[[Divergent Evolution]]**: Related species evolve different traits due to different environments.
* **[[Convergent Evolution]]**: Unrelated species evolve similar traits because they occupy similar ecological niches.
* **[[Coevolution]]**: Joint evolution of two interacting species (e.g., flowering plants and pollinators).
* **[[Extinction]]**: The complete disappearance of a species from Earth.

The pace of evolutionary change is modeled by **[[Gradualism]]** (slow, steady change) and **[[Punctuated Equilibrium]]** (long periods of stasis interrupted by brief, rapid change).
