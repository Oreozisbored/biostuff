# ⚛️ Lesson 2: The Chemical Foundations of Biological Systems

Life is a complex set of chemical reactions occurring within a aqueous medium. To understand biology, we must first study the properties of atoms, the nature of **[[Chemical Bonds]]**, the unique characteristics of **[[Water Properties]]**, and the structure and function of organic macromolecules.

---

## 🧪 Atoms, Elements, and Chemical Bonds

All matter is made of atoms. **[[Atoms and Elements]]** represent the basic units of chemistry:
* Atoms consist of a nucleus containing protons (positive charge) and neutrons (neutral charge), surrounded by an electron cloud containing electrons (negative charge).
* The arrangement of these electrons, particularly in the outermost shell, is known as the **[[Electron Configuration]]**. This configuration determines how reactive an atom is.
* Atoms with unstable electron configurations form bonds to achieve stability. **[[Isotopes]]** of elements have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons; radioactive isotopes are useful tools in biological research.

### 🔗 Types of Chemical Bonds
Atoms bind together to form an **[[Organic Molecules|Organic Molecule]]** or compound via:
1. **Covalent Bonds**: Formed when atoms share electrons. If atoms share electrons equally, it is a nonpolar covalent bond. If one atom is highly electronegative and pulls the electrons closer to itself, it forms a **[[Covalent Bonds|Polar Covalent Bond]]**.
2. **Ionic Bonds**: Formed when one atom transfers electrons to another, creating oppositely charged ions that attract each other (e.g., $NaCl$).
3. **Hydrogen Bonds**: Weak electrostatic attractions between a partially positive hydrogen atom in one polar molecule and a partially negative atom (usually oxygen or nitrogen) in another polar molecule.

---

## 💧 The Life-Giving Properties of Water

Water is the solvent of life. The oxygen-hydrogen bonds in water are polar, making water a polar molecule capable of forming hydrogen bonds. This configuration results in several critical **[[Water Properties]]**:
* **[[Cohesion and Adhesion]]**: Cohesion is water molecules sticking to each other (creating surface tension); adhesion is water sticking to other surfaces. Together, they enable capillary action, pulling water up vascular tissues in tall trees.
* **High Specific Heat**: Water requires a large amount of heat energy to raise its temperature because heat must first break hydrogen bonds before molecules can move faster. This stabilizes internal body temperatures and global climates.
* **Density of Ice**: Solid ice is less dense than liquid water because hydrogen bonds lock molecules into a spacious crystalline lattice. Floating ice sheets insulate aquatic life below from freezing temperatures.
* **Solvent Properties**: As a polar solvent, water forms hydration shells around polar molecules and ions, dissolving them and facilitating biological reactions.

---

## ⚖️ Solutions, Acids, and Bases

Water molecules occasionally dissociate into hydrogen ions ($H^+$) and hydroxide ions ($OH^-$). The balance between these ions in a solution is the domain of **[[Acids and Bases]]**:
* **Acids** increase the concentration of $H^+$ ions.
* **Bases** decrease the concentration of $H^+$ ions (often by releasing $OH^-$).
* The **[[pH Scale]]** is a logarithmic scale ranging from 0 to 14. A neutral solution has a pH of 7. Each unit change represents a 10-fold change in $H^+$ concentration.
* Living systems must maintain a stable pH. They utilize **[[Buffers]]**—weak acid-base pairs that accept or donate $H^+$ ions to resist sudden fluctuations in pH.

## 🧱 The Macromolecules of Life

Living organisms are built from carbon-based molecules because carbon's four valence electrons allow it to form complex, stable backbones. These backbones are decorated with specific **[[Functional Groups]]** (like hydroxyl, carboxyl, amino, and phosphate groups) that determine chemical behavior.

Macromolecules are polymers built from smaller subunits. They are created and broken down through:
* **[[Dehydration Synthesis]]**: Combining **[[Monomers and Polymers|Monomers]]** by removing a water molecule.
* **[[Hydrolysis]]**: Breaking down polymers into monomers by adding a water molecule.

### 1. Carbohydrates
**[[Carbohydrates]]** serve as energy sources and structural components:
* **[[Monosaccharides]]**: Simple sugars (e.g., glucose, fructose) containing 3 to 7 carbons.
* **Polysaccharides**: Long polymers of monosaccharides. Examples include starch (energy storage in plants), glycogen (energy storage in animals), cellulose (cell walls in plants), and chitin (cell walls in fungi and exoskeletons in arthropods).

### 2. Lipids
**[[Lipids]]** are hydrophobic, nonpolar hydrocarbons:
* **[[Fatty Acids]]**: Long carbon chains with a carboxyl group. Saturated fatty acids have single bonds and pack tightly (solid at room temperature); unsaturated fatty acids contain double bonds, creating kinks that prevent tight packing (liquid at room temperature).
* **[[Phospholipids]]**: Composed of a glycerol backbone, two fatty acid tails (hydrophobic), and a phosphate head (hydrophilic). This amphipathic nature makes them ideal for building membrane bilayers.
* **[[Steroids]]**: Lipids with four fused carbon rings (e.g., cholesterol, which regulates membrane fluidity and acts as a hormone precursor).

### 3. Proteins
**[[Proteins]]** are polymers of **[[Amino Acids]]** linked by peptide bonds:
* **[[Protein Structure]]** is organized into four levels:
  1. **Primary**: The unique linear sequence of amino acids in a polypeptide chain.
  2. **Secondary**: Coils and folds ($lpha$-helices and $eta$-pleated sheets) stabilized by hydrogen bonds along the backbone.
  3. **Tertiary**: The overall 3D shape of a single polypeptide, stabilized by R-group interactions (hydrophobic interactions, hydrogen bonds, ionic bonds, disulfide bridges).
  4. **Quaternary**: The assembly of multiple polypeptide subunits (e.g., hemoglobin).
* Protein structure is highly sensitive. Exposure to high heat, extreme pH, or high salt causes **[[Denaturation]]**, wherein a protein loses its shape and function.

### 4. Nucleic Acids
**[[Nucleic Acids]]** store and transmit genetic information. Composed of **[[Nucleotides]]** (each consisting of a pentose sugar, a nitrogenous base, and a phosphate group), they exist as **[[DNA]]** (double-stranded, stores genetic blueprints) and **[[RNA]]** (single-stranded, translates those blueprints into proteins).

## ⚡ Enzymes and Bioenergetics

Cellular reactions require an initial input of energy to start, known as **[[Activation Energy]]**. Cells utilize **[[Enzymes]]**—biological catalysts made of protein—to speed up reactions.

Enzymes lower the activation energy by binding specific reactants, called substrates, at a specialized pocket called the active site. The binding induces a conformational change (induced fit) that strains chemical bonds, facilitating the reaction.

Enzymes activity must be tightly controlled via **[[Enzyme Regulation]]**:
* **Competitive Inhibition**: An inhibitor molecule binds directly to the active site, blocking the substrate.
* **Noncompetitive (Allosteric) Inhibition**: An inhibitor binds to another site, changing the enzyme's shape and disabling the active site.
* **Feedback Inhibition**: The end product of a metabolic pathway binds to and inhibits an enzyme early in the pathway, preventing resource waste.
