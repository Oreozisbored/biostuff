# 🌍 Lesson 11: Principles of Ecology and Ecosystem Dynamics

**[[Ecology]]** is the scientific study of the interactions between organisms and their environments. To understand these interactions, ecologists study life at various **[[Levels of Ecological Organization]]**:
* **[[Biosphere]]**: The global ecosystem.
* **[[Biome]]**: A regional ecosystem characterized by specific climate and vegetation types.
* **[[Ecosystem]]**: All organisms in an area along with the nonliving physical environment.
* **[[Community]]**: All the populations of different species living in a specific area.
* **[[Population]]**: A group of individuals of the same species in an area.
* **[[Habitat]]**: The physical environment where an organism lives.

Every environment contains **[[Biotic Factors]]** (living components like plants and animals) and **[[Abiotic Factors]]** (nonliving components like temperature, water, sunlight, and soil).

---

## 📈 Population Ecology

Populations are dynamic, characterized by **[[Population Density]]** (individuals per unit area) and **[[Population Dispersion]]** (clumping, uniform, or random patterns).

**[[Population Growth]]** is determined by births, deaths, and **[[Immigration and Emigration]]**:
* **[[Exponential Growth]]**: Under ideal conditions with unlimited resources, a population grows at an accelerating rate, producing a J-shaped curve.
* **[[Logistic Growth]]**: Real-world growth where resources are limited. Growth slows as the population approaches the **[[Carrying Capacity]]** ($K$), the maximum population size the environment can support, producing an S-shaped curve.

Growth is regulated by **[[Limiting Factors]]**:
* **[[Density-Dependent Factors]]**: Factors whose impact increases as population density rises (e.g., competition, predation, disease).
* **[[Density-Independent Factors]]**: Factors that affect populations regardless of density (e.g., natural disasters, weather).

Severe resource depletion can lead to a **[[Population Crash]]**. Organisms exhibit different survival strategies, visualized on **[[Survivorship Curves]]** (Type I, II, or III).

## 🤝 Community Ecology

Every species occupies a specific **[[Ecological Niche]]**, its functional role and resource use in the community:
* **[[Competition]]** occurs when niches overlap. The **[[Competitive Exclusion]]** principle states that two species competing for the exact same resources cannot coexist.
* **[[Predation]]**: One species (predator) kills and eats another (prey).
* **[[Keystone Species]]**: A species that has an exceptionally large effect on its community relative to its abundance; its removal can cause community collapse.

Species also interact through close physical relationships called **[[Symbiosis]]**:
* **[[Mutualism]]**: Both species benefit (e.g., pollinators and flowers).
* **[[Commensalism]]**: One species benefits, the other is unaffected.
* **[[Parasitism]]**: One species benefits, the other is harmed.

Communities change over time through **[[Succession]]**:
* **[[Primary Succession]]**: Occurs in lifeless areas with no soil (e.g., post-glacial retreats). **[[Pioneer Species]]** (like lichens) break down rock to create soil.
* **[[Secondary Succession]]**: Occurs in disturbed areas where soil remains (e.g., post-forest fires).

## 🔄 Ecosystem Energetics and Nutrient Cycling

Ecosystems require a constant input of energy but recycle nutrients:
* **[[Producers]]** (autotrophs) capture solar energy and make organic compounds.
* **[[Consumers]]** (heterotrophs) ingest organic matter.
* **[[Decomposers]]** break down dead organic matter, returning nutrients to the soil/air.

Energy flow is traced through a **[[Food Chain]]** or a complex network called a **[[Food Web]]**. Energy is transferred between **[[Trophic Levels]]**:
* **[[10 Percent Rule]]**: Only about 10% of the energy at one trophic level is transferred to the next. The remaining 90% is lost as heat or waste.
* This loss of energy is represented in ecological pyramids: the **[[Energy Pyramid]]**, **[[Biomass Pyramid]]**, and **[[Pyramid of Numbers]]**.

Nutrients cycle through ecosystems via **[[Biogeochemical Cycles]]**:
* **[[Water Cycle]]**: Evaporation, condensation, precipitation, transpiration.
* **[[Carbon Cycle]]**: Respiration, photosynthesis, fossil fuel combustion.
* **[[Nitrogen Cycle]]**: Atmospheric nitrogen is converted into usable forms by bacteria in a process called **[[Nitrogen Fixation]]**.
