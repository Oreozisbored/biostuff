# 🧬 Lesson 6: The Cell Cycle, Mitosis, and Meiosis

Cell division is the biological mechanism of reproduction, growth, and tissue repair. While prokaryotes divide simply via **[[Binary Fission]]**, eukaryotes utilize complex, highly regulated nuclear division pathways.

---

## ⏳ The Eukaryotic Cell Cycle

The **[[Cell Cycle]]** is the lifecycle of a eukaryotic cell, divided into Interphase and the M Phase:

### 1. Interphase (90% of cell life)
* **[[G1 Phase]]**: Active growth, protein synthesis, and organelle replication.
* **[[S Phase]]**: DNA replication. After replication, each chromosome consists of two identical **[[Sister Chromatids]]** joined at the **[[Centromere]]**. The DNA exists as loose, uncoiled **[[Chromatin]]** threads.
* **[[G2 Phase]]**: Final preparations for division, synthesis of tubulin for spindle fibers.
* **[[G0 Phase]]**: A non-dividing state where cells exit the cycle (e.g., mature neurons and muscle cells).

### 2. The M Phase (Mitosis and Cytokinesis)
* **[[Mitosis]]**: Division of the nucleus in **[[Somatic Cells]]** (diploid body cells). It produces two genetically identical diploid daughter cells.
* **[[Cytokinesis]]**: Division of the cytoplasm.

---

## 🔬 The Stages of Mitosis

Mitosis is a continuous process divided into five phases:
1. **[[Prophase]]**: Chromatin condenses into visible **[[Chromosomes]]**. The **[[Mitotic Spindle]]** (made of microtubules) begins to form. The nucleolus disappears.
2. **[[Mitosis|Prometaphase]]**: The nuclear envelope breaks down. Spindle microtubules attach to protein complexes on the centromeres called **[[Mitosis|Kinetochores]]**.
3. **[[Metaphase]]**: Chromosomes align along the equator of the cell, known as the **[[Mitosis|Metaphase Plate]]**.
4. **[[Anaphase]]**: Sister chromatids are pulled apart by spindle fibers toward opposite poles of the cell. Once separated, they are considered individual chromosomes.
5. **[[Telophase]]**: Nuclear envelopes reform around the two sets of chromosomes, which begin to uncoil back into chromatin.

Following telophase, **[[Cytokinesis]]** divides the cytoplasm. In animal cells, a contractile ring of actin microfilaments pinches the cell, forming a **[[Cytokinesis|Cleavage Furrow]]**. In plant cells, vesicles carry cell wall materials to the cell center, forming a **[[Cytokinesis|Cell Plate]]** that grows into a new cell wall.

---

## 🛑 Regulation of the Cell Cycle and Cancer

The cell cycle is regulated by internal signals at **[[Cell Cycle Checkpoints]]** (G1, G2, and M checkpoints):
* **[[Cell Cycle Checkpoints|Cyclins]]** (proteins that fluctuate in concentration) and **[[Cell Cycle Checkpoints|Cyclin-Dependent Kinases]]** (CDKs) form active complexes that push the cell past checkpoints.
* External signals, such as **[[Growth Factors]]**, stimulate cell division.
* If a cell loses checkpoint control due to mutation, it divides uncontrollably. This leads to **[[Cancer]]**, characterized by the formation of **[[Tumors]]** (which can be benign or invasive, malignant tumors).

---

## 🎲 Meiosis: Sexual Reproduction and Variation

**[[Meiosis]]** is a specialized nuclear division that occurs in germ cells to produce haploid **[[Gametes]]** (sperm and egg). Unlike mitosis, meiosis involves one round of DNA replication followed by two successive divisions (**[[Meiosis I]]** and **[[Meiosis II]]**).

### 1. Meiosis I (Separation of Homologous Pairs)
* **[[Prophase I]]**: Homologous chromosomes pair up gene-for-gene in a process called synapsis, forming a tetrad. Non-sister chromatids cross over and swap genetic material, a process called **[[Crossing Over]]** that creates new combinations of alleles.
* **[[Metaphase I]]**: Homologous pairs line up along the metaphase plate. The orientation of the maternal and paternal chromosomes is random, a process called **[[Independent Assortment]]** that contributes to genetic variation.
* **[[Anaphase I]]**: Homologous chromosomes are pulled to opposite poles (sister chromatids remain attached).
* **[[Meiosis I|Telophase I]]** and Cytokinesis: Two haploid cells are formed.

### 2. Meiosis II (Separation of Chromatids)
* Similar to mitosis, **[[Meiosis II|Prophase II]]**, **[[Meiosis II|Metaphase II]]**, **[[Meiosis II|Anaphase II]]**, and **[[Meiosis II|Telophase II]]** occur, separating sister chromatids.
* Meiosis results in four genetically unique haploid cells.

The processes of gamete production are called **[[Gametogenesis]]** (spermatogenesis in males, oogenesis in females).

---

## 📊 Summary of Division Types and Errors

A comparison is summarized in **[[Mitosis vs Meiosis]]** (highlighting the genetic identity and ploidy differences of daughter cells). **[[Diploid and Haploid]]** define cell ploidy ($2n$ vs $n$).

Chromosomes can be visualized using a **[[Karyotype]]**. Failures in spindle fiber separation during meiosis can cause chromosomes to fail to separate, a process called **[[Chromosomes|Nondisjunction]]**. This leads to gametes with abnormal numbers of chromosomes (**[[Chromosomes|Aneuploidy]]**), such as Down syndrome (Trisomy 21).
