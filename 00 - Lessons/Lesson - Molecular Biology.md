# 📜 Lesson 8: Molecular Genetics and Gene Expression

How is genetic information stored, replicated, and expressed in cells? This lesson traces the molecular machinery of **[[DNA]]**, the flow of genetic information, and how mutations alter traits.

---

## 🧪 Identifying DNA as the Genetic Material

Before we understood the double helix, several landmark experiments proved that DNA, not protein, carries genetic information:
* **[[Griffith Experiment]]**: Discovered a "transforming principle" that could transfer virulent traits between strain types of bacteria.
* **[[Avery Experiment]]**: Demonstrated that DNA was the specific molecule responsible for Griffith's transforming principle.
* **[[Hershey-Chase Experiment]]**: Used radioactive isotopes of sulfur (protein) and phosphorus (DNA) to track bacteriophage components, proving that DNA is the genetic material injected into host cells.

---

## 🧬 DNA Structure and Replication

DNA is a nucleic acid composed of **[[Nucleotides]]**. Each nucleotide has a deoxyribose sugar, a phosphate group, and one of four **[[Nitrogenous Bases]]** (Adenine, Thymine, Cytosine, Guanine).
* **[[DNA Structure]]**: Two polynucleotide chains wound into a double helix. The sides of the helix form a **[[Sugar-Phosphate Backbone]]**, while the rungs consist of nitrogenous bases held together by hydrogen bonds.
* **[[Base Pairing Rules]]**: Adenine always pairs with Thymine ($A-T$), and Cytosine always pairs with Guanine ($C-G$).

During the S phase of the cell cycle, DNA replicates via **[[Semiconservative Replication]]**, meaning each new DNA double helix consists of one parental strand and one newly synthesized strand.
* **[[Helicase]]** unwinds and unzips the double helix, creating a replication fork.
* **[[DNA Polymerase]]** adds complementary nucleotides to the growing new strand. Because DNA polymerase can only add nucleotides to the 3' end, replication differs on the two strands:
  * The **[[Leading and Lagging Strand]]** arise because replication is continuous on the leading strand but discontinuous on the lagging strand.
  - The lagging strand is synthesized in short pieces called **[[Okazaki Fragments]]**.
  * **[[DNA Ligase]]** welds these Okazaki fragments together.

## 📜 The Central Dogma: DNA to Protein

The flow of genetic information is described by the **[[Central Dogma]]**: DNA is transcribed into **[[RNA]]**, which is then translated into protein.

### 1. Transcription (DNA to RNA)
Occurs in the nucleus of eukaryotes.
* **[[RNA Polymerase]]** binds to a DNA sequence called a **[[Promoter]]**.
* RNA polymerase unwinds the DNA and synthesizes a complementary strand of **[[mRNA]]** (messenger RNA) until it reaches a **[[Terminator]]** sequence.
* **[[RNA Processing]]**: In eukaryotes, pre-mRNA is modified before leaving the nucleus. Non-coding regions called **[[Introns and Exons]]** are modified: **introns** are spliced out, and **exons** are joined together. A 5' cap and a poly-A tail are also added.

### 2. Translation (RNA to Protein)
Occurs at **[[Ribosomes]]** in the cytoplasm.
* The ribosome reads the mRNA sequence in triplets of nucleotides called **[[Codons]]**.
* The relationship between codons and amino acids is governed by the **[[Genetic Code]]**, which starts at a specific **[[Start Codon]]** (AUG, coding for methionine) and ends at one of three **[[Stop Codons]]**.
* **[[tRNA]]** (transfer RNA) molecules act as translators. Each tRNA has an **[[Anticodon]]** at one end that pairs with the mRNA codon, and a corresponding amino acid at the other.
* The ribosome links the amino acids together with **[[Peptide Bond|Peptide Bonds]]** to form a polypeptide chain.
* **[[rRNA]]** (ribosomal RNA) makes up the physical structure of the ribosome and catalyzes peptide bond formation.

## ⚠️ Mutations: Altering the Genetic Message

**[[Mutations]]** are changes in the nucleotide sequence of DNA. They can occur spontaneously or be caused by environmental agents called **[[Mutagens]]**.
* **[[Point Mutations]]** involve changes in a single nucleotide pair:
  * **[[Silent Mutation]]**: Changes a codon but does not alter the amino acid produced (due to the redundancy of the genetic code).
  * **[[Missense Mutation]]**: Changes a codon so that it codes for a different amino acid.
  * **[[Nonsense Mutation]]**: Changes an amino acid codon into a stop codon, terminating translation prematurely.
* **[[Frameshift Mutations]]**: Involve insertions or deletions of nucleotides not in multiples of three. This shifts the reading frame of the genetic message, altering every amino acid downstream and usually resulting in a nonfunctional protein.
