# 🌳 Lesson 13: Taxonomy and the Diversity of Life

To make sense of the millions of species on Earth, biologists use **[[Taxonomy]]** to classify and name organisms based on evolutionary relationships. The system of naming uses **[[Binomial Nomenclature]]** (developed by Carl Linnaeus), giving each species a two-part scientific name: *Genus species*.

---

## 🗺️ The Three Domains of Life

All cellular life is classified into **[[Domains of Life]]**:
1. **[[Domain Bacteria]]**: Unicellular prokaryotes with peptidoglycan in their cell walls.
2. **[[Domain Archaea]]**: Unicellular prokaryotes that lack peptidoglycan and live in extreme environments.
3. **[[Domain Eukarya]]**: All organisms with eukaryotic cells.

---

## 👾 Acellular Entities (Viruses, Viroids, Prions)

While not considered alive, acellular agents interact heavily with cellular life:
* **[[Viruses]]**: Genetic material (DNA or RNA) enclosed in a protein coat. They are obligate intracellular parasites. A virus that infects bacteria is a **[[Bacteriophage]]**.
  * **[[Lytic Cycle]]**: The virus replicates rapidly inside the host and bursts (lyses) the cell to release new viruses.
  * **[[Lysogenic Cycle]]**: The viral DNA integrates into the host chromosome (as a prophage) and replicates silently along with the host cell.
* **[[Retroviruses]]**: RNA viruses (like HIV) that use reverse transcriptase to copy their RNA genome into DNA.
* **[[Viroids]]**: Tiny, naked molecules of circular RNA that infect plants.
* **[[Prions]]**: Uniquely infectious, misfolded proteins that cause degenerative brain diseases (like Mad Cow disease).

---

## 🧫 Eukaryotic Diversity

Under Domain Eukarya, life is traditionally split into kingdoms:
* **[[Protists]]**: A diverse, mostly unicellular group of eukaryotes that don't fit into plants, animals, or fungi (e.g., amoebas, **[[Protozoa]]**, and plant-like **[[Algae]]**). **[[Slime Molds]]** are fungus-like protists.
* **[[Fungi]]**: Heterotrophic decomposers with cell walls made of chitin. Their bodies are made of thread-like filaments called **[[Hyphae]]**, which form a mass called a **[[Mycelium]]**.
  * Fungi form mutualistic relationships: **[[Lichens]]** (symbiosis with algae/cyanobacteria) and **[[Mycorrhizae]]** (symbiosis with plant roots).
* **[[Plants Overview]]**: Multicellular, eukaryotic, autotrophs with cell walls of cellulose. Major plant groups include:
  * **[[Bryophytes]]**: Non-vascular, seedless plants (e.g., mosses).
  * **[[Seedless Vascular Plants]]**: Contain vascular tissue but do not produce seeds (e.g., ferns).
  * **[[Gymnosperms]]**: Vascular, non-flowering seed plants (e.g., conifers).
  * **[[Angiosperms]]**: Vascular, flowering seed plants.
* **[[Animals Overview]]**: Multicellular, eukaryotic, heterotrophs that lack cell walls. They are divided into **[[Invertebrates]]** (lack a backbone) and **[[Vertebrates]]** (have a backbone):
  * **[[Sponges]]**: Simplest animals; lack true tissues.
  * **[[Cnidarians]]**: Radial symmetry, stinging cells (e.g., jellyfish, corals).
  * **[[Flatworms]]**, Nematodes (roundworms), and **[[Annelids]]** (segmented worms).
  * **[[Molluscs]]**: Soft bodies, often with shells (e.g., snails, octopuses).
  * **[[Arthropods]]**: Jointed appendages, exoskeleton of chitin (e.g., insects, spiders).
  * **[[Echinoderms]]**: Radial symmetry as adults, spiny skins (e.g., starfish).
  * **[[Chordates]]**: Animals that have a notochord, dorsal hollow nerve cord, pharyngeal slits, and post-anal tail at some point in development (includes all vertebrates).
