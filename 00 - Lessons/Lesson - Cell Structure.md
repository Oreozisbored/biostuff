# 🧫 Lesson 3: Eukaryotic and Prokaryotic Cell Biology

The cell is the basic structural, functional, and biological unit of all living organisms. The foundational principles of cell biology are codified in the **[[Cell Theory]]**:
1. All living organisms are composed of one or more cells.
2. The cell is the basic unit of structure and organization in organisms.
3. Cells arise from pre-existing cells.

---

## 📏 Limitations on Cell Size

Cells are microscopic. This limitation is dictated by physical constraints, specifically the **[[Cell Size]]** surface area-to-volume ratio:
* As a cell increases in size, its volume grows cubically ($r^3$), while its surface area only grows quadratically ($r^2$).
* Since the membrane must transport all nutrients in and waste out, a large cell would have too much volume and too little surface area to support its metabolic demands.
* Keeping cells small ensures a high surface area-to-volume ratio, facilitating rapid diffusion and transport.

---

## 🌳 The Divergence of Life: Prokaryotes vs. Eukaryotes

All cells fall into one of two structural categories:
* **[[Prokaryotic Cells]]**: Found in domains Bacteria and Archaea. They are small, simple, lack a nucleus (DNA is concentrated in a non-membrane-bound region called the nucleoid), and lack membrane-bound organelles.
* **[[Eukaryotic Cells]]**: Found in domain Eukarya. They are larger, contain a membrane-enclosed nucleus containing DNA, and feature a compartmentalized cytoplasm filled with membrane-bound organelles.
* The differences and details between these are further evaluated in **[[Plant vs Animal Cells]]** (which details plant cell walls, central vacuoles, and chloroplasts versus animal centrioles and lysosomes).

## 🏗️ Inside the Eukaryotic Cell: Organelle Function

A eukaryotic cell is partitioned into specialized compartments to allow incompatible chemical reactions to occur simultaneously:

### 1. The Genetic Command Center
* **[[Nucleus]]**: Houses the cell's DNA in the form of chromatin. It is bounded by a double-membrane nuclear envelope containing pores that regulate transport. The **nucleolus** is a region inside the nucleus where ribosomes are assembled.
* **[[Ribosomes]]**: Non-membrane-bound complexes of rRNA and proteins that translate mRNA into polypeptides. They can float freely in the cytoplasm or be bound to the endoplasmic reticulum.

### 2. The Endomembrane System
This system is an interconnected network of membranes that synthesize, modify, package, and transport proteins and lipids:
* **[[Endoplasmic Reticulum]]**: Bounded sheets of membrane extending from the nuclear envelope. The **Rough ER** is studded with ribosomes and is the site of membrane and secretory protein synthesis. The **Smooth ER** lacks ribosomes and is responsible for lipid synthesis, carbohydrate metabolism, and drug detoxification.
* **[[Golgi Apparatus]]**: A stack of flattened membrane sacs (cisternae) that acts as the shipping and receiving center of the cell. It receives proteins from the ER in transport vesicles, modifies them, sorts them, and packages them into new **[[Vesicles]]** for transport.
* **[[Lysosomes]]**: Membrane-bound sacs filled with hydrolytic enzymes that break down damaged organelles, cellular waste, and food particles engulfed by the cell.
* **[[Vacuoles]]**: Large membrane storage compartments. Plant cells feature a giant central vacuole that stores water, regulates waste, and provides structural turgor pressure.

### 3. The Energy Converters
* **[[Mitochondria]]**: The powerhouses of the cell. They utilize oxygen to perform cellular respiration, converting glucose energy into ATP. They feature a double membrane: the outer membrane is smooth, and the inner membrane is folded into cristae to maximize ATP synthesis surface area.
* **[[Chloroplasts]]**: Found in plants and algae. They perform photosynthesis, capturing light energy to build sugars. They contain stacks of thylakoids (grana) floating in a fluid stroma.
* According to the **[[Eukaryotic Cells|Endosymbiotic Theory]]**, mitochondria and chloroplasts originated as free-living prokaryotic cells that were engulfed by a larger ancestral eukaryotic cell, eventually establishing a permanent, symbiotic relationship. Evidence includes their double membranes, their own circular DNA, and their own prokaryotic-like ribosomes.

### 4. The Cytoskeletal Scaffold and Support
The **[[Cytoskeleton]]** is a network of protein fibers throughout the cytoplasm that maintains cell shape, anchors organelles, and enables cell motility:
* **[[Microtubules]]**: Thick, hollow tubes made of tubulin. They form tracks for vesicle movement, make up the spindle fibers that separate chromosomes, and form the internal structure of **[[Centrioles]]** and moving appendages like cilia and flagella.
* **[[Microfilaments]]**: Thin, solid rods made of actin. They are responsible for muscle contraction, amoeboid movement, and the cleavage furrow that divides animal cells.
* **[[Intermediate Filaments]]**: Mid-sized, fibrous proteins (like keratin) that provide structural stability and anchor the nucleus in place.

### 5. Cell Boundaries and External Structures
* **[[Cell Wall]]**: A rigid exterior layer found in plants (made of cellulose), fungi (chitin), and bacteria. It prevents excessive water uptake and provides structural support.
* **[[Extracellular Matrix]]**: A meshwork of glycoproteins (such as collagen) surrounding animal cells, which lacks a cell wall. It coordinates cellular signaling, attachment, and tissue organization.
