# 🔄 Lesson 4: Membrane Transport and Homeostasis

Every cell is enveloped by a selectively permeable plasma membrane. This barrier keeps the cell's internal environment stable, regulating the entry of nutrients and the exit of waste products.

---

## 🎨 Membrane Structure: The Fluid Mosaic Model

The structure of the plasma membrane is described by the **[[Fluid Mosaic Model]]**:
* The membrane is a "mosaic" of proteins embedded in or attached to a fluid lipid bilayer.
* The primary matrix is a **[[Phospholipid Bilayer]]**. Each phospholipid is amphipathic, containing a hydrophilic (water-loving) phosphate head and two hydrophobic (water-fearing) fatty acid tails.
* The bilayer arranges itself spontaneously in water with heads facing outward and tails tucked inward.
* **[[Membrane Proteins]]** are embedded in this bilayer. They function as transport channels, enzymes, cell receptors, and structural attachments.
* Cholesterol molecules are interspersed in the bilayer to regulate membrane fluidity across different temperatures.
* Carbohydrates attached to lipids (glycolipids) or proteins (glycoproteins) on the outer surface act as cellular identification tags.
* This configuration results in **[[Selective Permeability]]**: hydrophobic, nonpolar molecules (like $O_2$ and $CO_2$) pass through easily, while hydrophilic, polar molecules (like glucose, water, and ions) require transport proteins.

---

## 📉 Passive Transport: Diffusion and Osmosis

Passive transport is the movement of substances across the membrane without any input of cellular energy. It occurs down a **[[Concentration Gradient]]** (from high to low concentration):
* **[[Passive Transport]]**: General movement of solutes down their gradient.
* **[[Diffusion]]**: The net movement of particles from high to low concentration due to random molecular motion.
* **[[Facilitated Diffusion]]**: The passive movement of polar molecules and ions across the membrane using specialized channel or carrier proteins. For example, water crosses the membrane through specialized channel proteins called aquaporins.
* **[[Osmosis]]**: The diffusion of water across a selectively permeable membrane.

---

## ⚖️ Tonicity and Water Balance

Water movement across membranes is governed by **[[Tonicity]]**—the relative solute concentration of a cell's cytoplasm compared to its surrounding solution:
* **[[Isotonic Solution]]**: The solute concentration is equal. Water flows in and out at equal rates. Animal cells are healthy; plant cells become **[[Tonicity|Flaccid]]** (lacking turgor).
* **[[Hypotonic Solution]]**: The solution has a lower solute concentration than the cell. Water rushes into the cell. Animal cells swell and burst (**[[Tonicity|Lysis]]**). Plant cells accumulate water inside their vacuoles, pushing against the rigid cell wall to create **[[Tonicity|Turgor Pressure]]**, which is the healthy, upright state for plants.
* **[[Hypertonic Solution]]**: The solution has a higher solute concentration than the cell. Water rushes out of the cell. Animal cells shrivel. In plant cells, the plasma membrane pulls away from the cell wall, a lethal process called **[[Tonicity|Plasmolysis]]**.

---

## ⚡ Active Transport: Pumping Against the Gradient

When cells need to accumulate substances against their concentration gradient (from low to high concentration), they must perform **[[Active Transport]]**, which requires energy input (ATP):
* **[[Active Transport]]** utilizes carrier proteins that act as ATP-powered pumps.
* The **[[Sodium-Potassium Pump]]** is a critical active transport mechanism in animal cells. It pumps three sodium ions ($Na^+$) out of the cell for every two potassium ions ($K^+$) pumped in, using ATP. This generates an electrochemical gradient across the membrane.
* A **[[Active Transport|Proton Pump]]** pumps hydrogen ions ($H^+$) across a membrane, generating a voltage and concentration gradient. This gradient can power the active transport of other nutrients (like sucrose) against their gradients through a coupled transport process known as **[[Active Transport|Cotransport]]**.

---

## 📦 Bulk Transport: Endocytosis and Exocytosis

Large macromolecules (like proteins and polysaccharides) cannot fit through transport proteins. They are moved across the membrane in membrane-bound vesicles:
* **[[Exocytosis]]**: Vesicles fuse with the plasma membrane, releasing their contents outside the cell (used to secrete hormones and neurotransmitters).
* **[[Endocytosis]]**: The cell takes in substances by folding the plasma membrane inward to form a new vesicle:
  * **[[Phagocytosis]]**: "Cell eating"—the cell engulfs large, solid particles or microorganisms (e.g., white blood cells engulfing bacteria).
  * **[[Pinocytosis]]**: "Cell drinking"—the non-specific engulfment of extracellular fluid droplets.
  * **[[Receptor-Mediated Endocytosis]]**: Highly selective uptake. Specific extracellular molecules (ligands) bind to receptors on the membrane, triggering vesicle formation.

Wait, cells also use **[[Signal Transduction]]** pathways, where receptors on the membrane receive external molecular signals and transmit them inside the cell to trigger physiological responses.
